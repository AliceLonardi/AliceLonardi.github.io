TOPIC OF THE WEEK: THE FOOD SYSTEM

Last week we learned about the absurd food situation in our world: people who need food are not getting it and food that is getting wasted is heating up our planet. We don’t need to go far to see the effects of it: In Boulder 40% of food is wasted while 12.8% of Boulder County residents are food insecure. Still, food waste is just the tip of the iceberg when it comes to the problems of the food system.

When you think of the causes of global warming, the first thing that comes to mind is probably fossil fuel. Way less evident are the consequences of breakfast, lunch and dinner (and snacks!). We are used going to the grocery store and have very convenient access to large volumes and huge varieties of food, but the environmental impacts of this availability have been severe.

The food system is elaborate and complex and its requirements and impacts are extraordinary. Fossil fuels are powering every step of the food process, from farm to kitchen. Chemical fertilizers are commonly used in agriculture and are a problem for our health and the health of our planet. The production of meat involves over 60 billion land animals, which require nearly half of all agricultural land for food and pasture, and it is responsible for 18 to 20 percent of emitted greenhouse gases annually, second only to fossil fuel. Large-scale agriculture is linked to water pollution, soil loss, habitat destruction, loss of biodiversity and many other problems. Food transportation is highly inefficient: The food eaten in the United States typically travels approximately 1,500 miles (over 2400 km) from farm to table. Considering all food related emissions, what we eat turns out to be the first cause of global warming along with the energy sector! Additionally, there are many places along our food chain where people’s vulnerabilities are exploited and where injustice and inequity turn up, including the pay level and rights of farm workers, the exploitation of the communities that live close to industrial farms, the inequity in access to healthy, nourishing and sustainable produces food.

How did we get at this level? Let’s go through a very short history of the food system in the U.S.

In 1900, food was hardly ever shipped - the exception being oranges at Christmas. There was no frozen food and restaurants were much less common, which means people cooked their food at home. As suburbs began to develop in the 1930’s, the family farm began to decline. Large farms became more efficient with advancing technology, allowing businesses to accumulate surpluses of food. Frozen and canned foods began being used to feed urban populations. The completion of the new highway system in 1956 allowed large amounts of food to be zipped across the country, allowing food to be sold miles away from where it was grown. Pre-made foods also represented new freedom to the housewives of the time who previously spent so much time cooking. In the 1950’s, the US population exploded, meat consumption grew fivefold, and the quality of home cooked meals began to decline. Industrial-scale, factory-style farms began to put small farmers out of business. In the 1970’s, some people had realized that factory farming was having negative side effects on our food and our environment. Thus, the food industry started emphasizing local and organic ingredients. While some people have embraced natural foods, many more continue to eat fast food and pre-made meals. Today we have a larger industrial food system than ever before.

Can WE do something about it? As said before, don’t let the topic discourage you! There are solutions out there and there are so many people already contributing to developing and effectively implementing those solutions at all stages of the food system! This includes alternative production methods (cultivating organic produce, using more sustainable methods and regenerating our soil doing so, producing meat in a more ethical and sustainable way, labels that certify those alternatives), more direct and local methods to distribute food (like community sustained agriculture (CSA) programs or farmers markets), and finally more and more conscious consumers (ALL OF US) that are making a difference with their choices. Every single drop is contributing to stop the fire and the more drops the easier it is! In the Tips of the week part I am giving the 5 most effective actions that you can take to make a difference with your food choices. In the next few weeks we are going to consider in more details some specific topic and we will take a look at the solutions that manage to bring together sustaining the planet’s living systems, addressing food security and agricultural resilience, contributing to a more robust food system and protecting human health, all inextricably linked.

TIPS OF THE WEEK

Do you want to make a difference with your food choices? Here are 5 of the most effective ways to do so! If you have specific questions on this topic, write me an email!

  1. Know your Source. The first step to an environmental awareness about what you eat is to know who grew it and how!
  2. Buy local: consider farmers market, farm stands, CSA programs. Boulder is an amazing place for this kind of possibilities!
  3. Buy organic. Buying organic is always the best option, but sometimes we can’t afford to go all out. Be aware that there are differences between produce, depending on the amount of pesticides they require and the absorption rate for those pesticides! Fortunately, buying non-organic produce of some kind of vegetables and fruits is relatively safe (in Colorado this is valid for onions, sweet corn, pineapples, avocado, asparagus, sweet peas, mangoes, eggplants, cantaloupe, kiwi, cabbage, watermelons, sweet potatoes, grapefruits, mushrooms), while you really want to avoid buying non-organic for others (again, in Colorado this is valid for apples, celery, strawberries, peaches, spinach, nectarines, grapes, sweet bell peppers, potatoes, blueberries, lettuce, kale).
  4. Eat in season. Foods purchased out of season require either long distance transportation from someplace that is in season or resource intensive practices to extend the growing time. Don’t know what is in season? For a list of the growing periods for food Colorado, check out this website. 
  5. Buy Fresh Ingredients. Processed food can have enhanced flavors and preservatives that make foods taste good for longer periods of time, but lots of processed foods lack nutrition and come from factory-style monocultures. Additionally, processed foods are placed in plastic containers for easy distribution and protection. By buying fresh food, you can avoid negative impacts on your health and the environment.

GOOD NEWS OF THE WEEK

Many innovative programs are seeking to support Native American communities in becoming more food-sovereign and self-reliant. Across the country, as households turn to planting and gardening as a relaxing hobby or to become more self-sufficient during turbulent times, the act of cultivating one's own food has taken on a greater significance. That's especially true among Native American communities, where the pandemic has laid bare an enduring food crisis and a desire to return to customs and traditions that some fear are slowly being lost. Native seed organizations say the pandemic has increased interest in what they've been doing for decades. Read more about the program here.
