TOPIC OF THE WEEK: POLLINATORS

A pollinator is anything that helps pollen travel from the male part of the flower to the female part of the same or another flower. It can literally be anything: Wind and water are included in the list of pollinators, together with a large variety of insects, like bees, but also wasps, moths, butterflies, beetles, and flies. Other animals, such as birds, bats and other small mammals can also be pollinators. Some of those animals depend on flowers for food, and some of them (like the bees) specifically collect pollen, while others look for flowers as shelter, nest-building materials, or just have their favorite spot close to flowers. In all cases, pollen sticks on their bodies and ends up being transported from flower to flower resulting in pollination. This movement of pollen must occur for the plant to become fertilized and produce fruits, seeds, and young plants.

This is such an important process, it is calculated that at least 75% of all the flowering plants on earth are pollinated by insects and animals! Pollinators are fundamental for the environment, and for us: One third of all food we eat exists because of the efforts of living pollinators, including many fruits, vegetables, and seeds. This is valid for the food that other animals eat too! The importance of pollinators is not limited to food production, either: without fruit, there would be no seed. And without seeds, there would be no new plants. Pollinators are key to the reproduction of crops, as well as wild plants, all of which keep the ecosystems healthy!

Why do we automatically think about bees when talking about pollinators? Bees are indeed one of the most effective pollinators: They spend their days specifically seeking pollen and nectar and their bodies are full of hairs, increasing the likelihood of moving pollen from flower to flower. Additionally, they are relatively abundant compared to the other insect pollinators. Bees (both wild and domestic) perform about 80% of all pollination worldwide. Seventy out of the top 100 human food crops are pollinated by them, including fruits, vegetables and nuts.

But let’s be clear: Bees, and especially one type, the honeybees, have an amazing PR office! If we talk about bees, what most of us think about is one specific insect: the black and yellow striped one that lives in hives and is pictured in numerous cartoons, children books, and even cereal boxes! Those bees are the European honeybee, and they are actually just one of the numerous bee species that exist. Worldwide, there are probably between 20,000 and 30,000 species of bee - more than all mammal, reptile, bird, fish, and amphibian species combined! In North America alone, there are about 4,000 bee species. Even in city parks, you can find more than 60 bee species and private gardens support up to 30 bee species!

Most of those bee species are defined as “wild bees” and they are the ones doing most of the pollination work. What makes “wild” bees different from honey bees? Here are some of the biggest differences: 

  • Most wild bees are solitary (so-called hermit bees)! 95% of all species do not live in colonies. The exception is the bumblebees, which live in small colonies of 50 to 600 animals. For comparison, Honeybee colonies can contain up to 50,000 individuals!
  • Most wild bees transport pollen on small devices on their legs, like the honey bees, but some of them use their belly hairs and others swallow it.
  • Most wild bees nest underground - about 70% of the species - while honeybees nest in hives.
  • Most wild bees only move 70 to 500 meters away from their nest, so they tend to nest where food sources are available. Honeybees can travel up to 7 kilometers, almost 4.5 miles, just to find food!
  • Honeybees produce a significant amount of honey that they use as supply for the winter. The only wild bee that keeps a honey supply is the bumblebee, even if in a way smaller amount compared to the honeybees. This is how honeybees got their name and also the main reason why they are bred by humans and became domestic. 
  • Different wild bees are adapted to different conditions. For example, some wild bees can survive lower temperatures compared to honeybees and are super important for plants that bloom early in the season.
  • Honeybees are generalist, which means that they use all available flowers. About 30% of wild bee species specialize in a few plants, living in symbiosis with them. And there are plants that are only pollinated by certain wild bees. One example of that is tomatoes: in tomato plants the pollen lies in elongated, closed anthers and the only species of bees that can open these “bags” is the bumblebee, using their flight muscles to generate vibrations that shake out the pollen. 

The amazing variety of bees is just one aspect of the incredible world of pollinators. About 200,000 species of animals act as pollinators. Of those, 1,000 are hummingbirds, bats, and small mammals such as mice. The rest are insects like beetles, bees, ants, wasps, butterflies and moths. All of them have very specific characteristic ways of pollinating. Did you know for example that there are more than 530 species of flowering plants that rely on bats as either their major or exclusive pollinators? Some of those plants are mangoes and bananas! Another amazing example of pollinators are biting midges, and those annoying insects are the exclusive pollinators of cocoa plants! Take a look at the additional resources, if you are interested in learning more about other pollinators!

Why is Pollinator Appreciation Month important? First of all, because pollinators are amazing and a vast amount of the food we eat depends on them! Second, because populations of bees and other pollinators are declining around the world. The publication of The Forgotten Pollinators by Stephen L. Buchman and Gary Paul Nabhan in 1997 first raised awareness about the decline of pollinators globally, especially tropical bat and bird species. More recently, the loss of honeybee colonies in the U.S. and parts of Europe due to colony collapse disorder (CCD) and the 2007 publication, Status of Pollinators in North America, has raised further concern. We should be careful in putting those together. Honeybees may be insects, but when domesticated, they function as livestock. The CCD is a severe agricultural problem, as many crop productions depend entirely on honeybee colonies. This is a phenomenon tightly connected with the problems of the food system we talked about in the last weeks: Growing monoculture practice, increase in pesticide use, and unhealthy hive density are some of the interconnected causes of the disappearing of bees. The decline in wild pollinator populations is an urgent biodiversity issue, and it might be even more worrisome than the honeybee decline. Some of the causes might be the same as the decline in honeybee population, like the intense use of pesticide. But deeper problems, like habitat loss, invasive non-native species, climate change, have an even stronger effect. While some of the plants that depend on honeybees can be artificially pollinated by humans, we cannot pollinate the whole world. The hidden consequences of specific types of pollinators might be more important than what we even know.

TIPS OF THE WEEK

Do you want to help pollinators out? Take a look at those simple suggestions for your garden/balcony:

  1. Provide habitat for different pollinators by planting native flowers that bloom at different times. Nectar-rich flowers attract both insect pollinators and hummingbirds.
  2. Help different types of pollinators planting flowers of different shapes, sizes, and colors. Also planting flowers in clumps, rather than scattering single flowers around makes it easier for pollinators to find their meal.
  3. Offer pollinators a drink! Place a shallow dish of water on your deck or window sill to help thirsty pollinators stay hydrated. By placing several semi-submerged stones in the water dish, you can provide pollinators with places to land so they can drink without running the risk of drowning.
  4. If you have a garden, limit the use of pesticides. If you don’t have a garden, consider buying organic produce, that involves a limited use of pesticides. Supporting organic farms, you indirectly support pollinators!
  5. Nesting and sheltering sites are needed! Remember that different types of pollinators require different things. Some bees and pollinating beetles may use downed tree limbs and logs; many butterflies lay eggs and rear their young on plants; a small patch of bare ground might serve ground-nesting bees. Potential nesting sites include trees (both living and dead), shrubs, brush piles, bare ground, and bee boxes. Even if all of this is not possible on your balcony, they are little steps that can help a lot! You can take a look at some of those websites for ideas on how to build a pollinator house, or how to make a simple upcycled bee hotel, or a hummingbird feeder with a water bottle or other recycled material.

GOOD NEWS OF THE WEEK

'Sweet City': The Costa Rican Suburb that Gave Citizenship to Bees, Plants and Trees

San Jose, Costa Rica, suburb Curridabat is serving as a model for ecosystems and promoting environmental health and sustainability. Referred to as "Ciudad Dulce," or "Sweet City", Curridabat provides ecosystem services to residents, prioritizing green space and planting native species. Magalli Castro lvarez, who oversees Costa Rica's network of biocorridors with the National System of Conservation Areas, said, "Biocorridors improve air quality, water quality and give people spaces to relax, have fun and improve their health." Germany Takes Action to Save Insects

“Insects play an important role in the ecosystem … but in Germany, their numbers and their diversity has severely declined in recent years,” says the draft of a new law that the German environment hopes to pass by October. The changes put forward include stricter controls on night-time lighting and on the use of insecticides. The reforms are part of the German government’s more general “insect protection action plan," which was announced last September under increasing pressure from environmental and conservation activists. During the past year, Germany has been in the headlines with its efforts to protect insects. Last April, the state government of Bavaria was caught off guard by a wildly popular petition calling for greater protection for bees. So, instead of putting the petition to a referendum, the state simply passed it straight into law after 1.75 million people signed in a matter of months.
