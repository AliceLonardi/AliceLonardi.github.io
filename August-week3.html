TOPIC OF THE WEEK: THE CROP PRODUCTION

Last week we started to dive deeper into meat production, one of the many issues connected to the food system. Industrialized meat production is one of the biggest problems associated with industrial agriculture, which is currently the dominant food production system in the United States. Is eliminating meat from our diets the magic solution to all of our environmental problems? Not completely. Industrial crop production is the other side of the same coin of industrial agriculture. This week we are going to take a look at why industrial crop production is problematic and see some of the amazing alternatives that are sprouting all over the world: Agriculture can either be a cause of global warming and a problem for our ecosystem, or it can be a sustainable practice and even a way to fight global warming. This could even be a part of the environmental solution, if done right!

Industrial crop production uses modern technology to promote faster growth, reduce illness and increase yield. This is how they create food products on a mass scale. It is also characterized by large-scale monoculture, heavy use of chemical fertilizers, pesticides, genetically modified organisms (GMOs), and intense use of fossil fuel powered machinery. This approach to farming is also defined by its heavy emphasis on a few crops that overwhelmingly end up as animal feed, biofuels, and processed junk food ingredients. 

There are some people who consider industrial agriculture to be one of the world’s greatest innovations. We often speak about the “Green Revolution” associated with all the technology transfer initiatives that increased agricultural production worldwide in the 1950s and 1960s: It included the development of high-yielding varieties of cereal grains, expansion of irrigation infrastructure, modernization of management techniques, and distribution of hybridized seeds, synthetic fertilizers, and pesticides to farmers. The American agronomist that is considered the father of the Green Revolution, Norman Ernest Borlaug, won the nobel peace prize in 1970. Those practices increased yield and lowered the costs of food production, making food cheaper for the consumers. For these reasons, industrial agriculture has often been sold to the public as a technological miracle. Its efficiency, we are told, would allow food production to keep pace with a rapidly growing global population, while its economies of scale would ensure that farming remained a profitable business.

Something crucial is left out of this story: This type of industrial food system has steep costs, a lot of which are left to be paid by rural communities, farmers themselves and future generations. Obviously the biggest cost comes to the environment. When we consider all of this, maybe this system is not as cost-effective or healthy as we are led to believe. Plus, it is definitely not sustainable. Let’s take a closer look at those downsides.

Damage to farmland and the rural environment. The soils of the American Corn Belt were once celebrated for their fertility. Often, that fertility is considered a resource to exploit, not to maintain. Monoculture exhausts soil fertility, requiring applications of chemical fertilizers. Additionally, it degrades soil structure and leaves it more vulnerable to erosion, resulting in the need of soil replacement, cleanup, and leads to the loss of farmland value. Soils used to grow annual crops are then left bare for much of the remainder of the year. This causes poor drought resistance and the need for increased irrigation. It is calculated that the US loses 3 tons of topsoil per acre, per year. Estimates say that there are 950 million to 1.1 billion acres of deserted farmland around the world! The land that was once used for crops or pasture has now been abandoned because it was “farmed out”. Industrial farms don't support the rich range of life that more diverse farms do. As a result, the land suffers from a shortage of the ecosystem services, such as pollination, that a more diverse landscape offers.

Costs to human health and safety. Industrial farming is bad for the health of workers, consumers, and downstream neighbors. How so? Herbicides and insecticides commonly used in agriculture have been associated with both acute poisoning and long-term chronic illness. The intense use of fertilizer is associated with water pollution, requiring costly cleanup measures with an annual price tag of nearly $2 billion. Industrial agriculture, especially in the central United States, mostly produces commodity crops like corn and soybeans, used to make the processed foods that dominate the US diet, with serious and enormously costly health impacts.

Social and economic impacts. The pressure to "get big or get out" is fundamental to industrial agriculture and takes a toll on communities. Once the backbone of US agriculture, medium-sized farms are more and more rare, which means that fewer and fewer people make their living as farmers. Currently, just 20 % of farms control nearly 70 % of US farmland. This has a heavy impact on the economies of rural communities and farm states. Industrial agriculture has intense effects on areas even very far away from the production sites: just ask local governments and utility managers who must install expensive equipment to remove fertilizer by-products from public drinking water supplies. Or ask people who make their living from fisheries or tourism on the Gulf of Mexico and elsewhere. In these areas more and more "dead zones" and toxic algae blooms are being caused by farm runoff. This damage comes with an annual price tag in the billions. 

Are there alternatives? Yes, a different way of producing crops exists! And it is not only a more sustainable way, but also reduces the damage we cause to the environment. In most cases it can become a way to improve soil fertility, clean water systems, biodiversity and other ecosystem services, rather than depleting them. “Sustainable agriculture” includes a broad range of different methods, like organic, agroecological, regenerative and permaculture methods. Most of them look quite different from a typical farm. This is a very basic overview:

  • Agroforestry: Integrates trees and shrubs into crop and animal farming, for the benefit of all species in the system.
  • Permaculture: A farming philosophy that integrates landscape and people according to holistic principles. 
  • Rooftop farms and other urban agriculture: Agriculture that brings food production closer to communities by growing on city rooftops, in small backyard plots and in vacant lots.
  • Agroecology: The application of ecological concepts to the design and management of sustainable agro-ecosystems – farming in cooperation with nature. Many farming practices like crop rotations and agroforestry, are agroecological practices. 
  • Regenerative: A farming philosophy and set of methods that go beyond organic by aiming to regenerate the air, soil, water, local environments and communities. Regenerative agriculture methods usually also include a philosophy of fairness and humane treatment to both people and animals.

A common argument against sustainable agriculture is that the world population is increasing and those methods cannot “feed the world”. At the moment, industrial agriculture produces 2,800 daily calories for every person on earth and we still have 1 in 9 people suffering from hunger worldwide. Feeding the world is a problem of power, not of calories, and industrial agriculture does not help in a better redistribution of “food” sovereignty. Additionally, research has shown that various kinds of sustainable agriculture produce similar yields as those of chemical-dependent farming, in some cases even greater (particularly in drought conditions, which is increasingly important as the climate changes).

TIPS OF THE WEEK

How can you personally make a difference concerning sustainable agriculture?

  1. Buy organic food. Organic standards exclude the use of synthetic fertilizers and most pesticides. As a result, there’s strong evidence that these methods can combat the causes of global climate change.
  2. Look for local farms that practice regenerative agriculture. One of them happens to be The Ollin Farms, the farm that offers the CSA program every year to Graduate and Family Housing! While it is too late to subscribe to their CSA program, you can buy their produce from their virtual farmstand weekly. Find more details about the farmstand in the “Events of the week” section (Food appointments), read about the farm and how they practice agriculture on their website and take a look at the panel discussion with KGNU where Mark and Kena talk about their way of farming!
  3. Buy Fresh Ingredients and cook more from scratch. Approximately 90% of manufactured foods are processed from just four items – wheat, rice, corn, and potatoes. Processed foods can have enhanced flavors and preservatives that make them taste good for longer periods of time. However, a lot of processed foods lack nutrition and come from factory-style monocultures. By buying more fresh food, you can limit negative impacts on your health and the environment.
  4. Get a garden plot and produce your own food. Citing Micheal Pollan in his article “Why bother?” in the New York Times, “planting a garden… is one of the most powerful things an individual can do – to reduce your carbon footprint, sure, but more important, to reduce your sense of dependence and dividedness: to change the cheap-energy mind.”

GOOD NEWS OF THE WEEK

Rafael Duckur, an organic farmer in Sao Paulo, Brazil, relies mostly on the community-supported agriculture (CSA) model, where his produce goes directly to consumers. While his customer base is strong, the pandemic has stopped his sales to schools and restaurants, and Ducker was disheartened to see surplus food going to waste, when so many are going hungry. Putting out a call on social media, he teamed up with a neighboring organic farmer, and together they initiated a network of farmers working to help families in the favelas in need of food. Supported by cash donations and "Robin Hood baskets" (whereby the purchase of two boxes enable a farmer to give one box for free), the network has distributed more than 400 boxes of food in four favelas.
