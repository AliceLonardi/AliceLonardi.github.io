Happy New Year Residents!

2020 has been a crazy year for all of us. Even if the year has been largely dominated by the news about COVID and the pandemic, a lot more happened. And the news about the environment has not been very positive. Fires, floods, hurricanes, droughts, deep-freezes… Climate instability has gotten even more scary and the consequences are visible all over the world. We don’t need to go far: All of us that have been in Boulder this fall have in mind the fires so close to home in October. And even if all of this can be overwhelming and scary, it can become our reason to push for change. The new year CAN be different and our contribution, even if small, can be fundamental for this change. 

In this email you can find suggestions for resolutions during the new year. They are small actions and lifestyle changes we can all make. If even a small part of the population did them, it would create a revolution and have a huge impact on the health of this fragile planet we love. “The ocean is made of drops”. I am also giving a list of events and nice things to do in Boulder for next week. At the bottom of the email you can find positive news meant to give hope: Some changes are already happening!

As usual, if you missed some of the old newsletters, you can find them in the Sustainability Newsletter Archive. And if you have any specific topics you would like to see in one of the next weeks, let me know! This is the email address: gfhsustainability@colorado.edu. Inputs and comments are always very welcome!

TOPIC OF THE WEEK: RESOLUTIONS FOR THE NEW YEAR

I am personally not a big fan of New Year’s resolutions: They are difficult to keep and most people abandon theirs by February. I do strongly believe in small changes that everyone can slowly implement in their life and turn into new habits. That is why the resolutions I am suggesting are little and very defined actions and my advice is to pick one or two of them and focus on making them yours for a few weeks before going for additional ones. The idea is to build more sustainable habits incrementally to help make them stick. Another tip is to involve your family or your flatmates and make these resolutions a challenge or a game with them. Working together with a common purpose helps building a friendship and makes the challenge easier and fun! Whatever you choose to go for, I am here to support you, so don’t hesitate to contact me!

1. Reduce plastic use

The world’s plastic problem is huge. 33 million tons of plastic waste per year is generated in the USA alone and only a small fraction of it is recycled. We talked extensively about this in one of the first newsletters I sent out last summer (and if you want additional resources on this topic, please feel free to write to me!). Here are some easy switches that you can start in 2021 to reduce the amount of plastic you use on a daily basis:

  • Say no to plastic bags and use a reusable bag (if you would like one, contact me or ask at the Graduate and Family Housing Office!)
  • Stop buying bottled water and refill your reusable bottle from the tap!
  • Say no to plastic straws and get a metal or glass one to keep with you!
  • Carry reusable utensils with you so you can refuse the single-use ones when you eat out (ask me for our amazing bamboo set if you don’t have one already!)
  • Swap out other disposables for a reusable alternative: Some great options are cloth rags and napkins instead of paper towels, washable facial rounds instead of the disposable ones, safety razor instead of single-use one, bamboo toothbrush instead of a plastic one. You can find many more options in the old newsletters, take a look at the archive!
  • Check out bulk stores in Boulder: Some fun possibilities are Refill Revolution and Conscious Merchant for personal care and cleaning products, Nude Food Market or Simply Bulk Market for grocery shopping, Ku Cha House of Tea for loose-leaf tea.
  • Consciously choose alternative packaging for the products you buy at the supermarket: Glass jars or metal cans are easier to recycle than plastic one and they are on the same shelf in the shops!
2. Reduce your food waste and make more sustainable food choices

In the U.S., up to 40% of food goes uneaten. Food waste produces 4.4 gigatons of carbon dioxide equivalent, roughly 8% of the total human greenhouse emissions. Considering all food related emissions, what we eat turns out to be the first cause of global warming along with the energy sector! Again, last summer I sent out an email focused on food waste and several on the food system. You can find all of them in the archive if you are interested in more information about this topic. Here are some easy ways to reduce your amount of food waste and make more sustainable food choices that can become your 2021 resolutions:

  • Try meal-planning and shopping with a list. 
  • Learn alternative recipes to get creative with your leftovers.
  • Start composting if you are not doing it already! Ask for a tabletop compost bin at the Graduate and Family Housing Office or directly to me!
  • Prioritize shopping locally: Consider the CSA (Community Supported Agriculture) program with Ollin Farms for next spring, subscriptions are already open! Ask me for more information if you are curious!
  • Choose organic and seasonal produce wherever possible 
  • Reduce your meat consumption and choose better meat for your meals.
3. Say no to fast fashion

If the concept of Fast Fashion is new to you, stay tuned for the newsletter on that topic (probably one of the next weeks!). In short, low-cost clothing has a high human and environmental cost and despite the fact that each item costs you less than high-quality clothing, it ends up costing you more in the long term. In 2021 focus on the clothes you buy:

  • Buy less. Fashion changes fast, but a lot of the clothes you buy are still good. Your personal style does not need to follow rules that change every two months and even older clothes can be combined or rearranged in very cool new ways.
  • Check second hand stores first. Thrift stores are an amazing source of “new” clothes and are an amazing alternative to the trash bin for clothes that you are not wearing anymore!
  • Buy more quality items that last longer. There are dozens of slow fashion brands dedicated to ethical and sustainable practices.
4. Drive less and consider transportation alternatives

This is going to be the main topic of one of the future newsletters. Again, stay tuned for all details! But here are some great changes that you can already consider as 2021 resolutions:

  • Avoid flying when possible and start carbon offsetting the travels you can’t avoid.
  • Drive less: Cars are convenient, but are one of the biggest environmental problems.
  • Bike and walk more: Additionally to the sustainability aspect, it is healthier, both for your body and your mind, and it is less expensive!
  • Check-out public transport alternative in Boulder: When biking and walking is complicated, buses are here to help. And most CU students and employees have an ECOPASS for free!
5. Save energy at home

Before Christmas we started to talk about ways to reduce our carbon footprint. Suggestions and more insights on this are going to be the topic of next week’s newsletter. Here are some practical tips that can already become your 2021 resolutions

  • Don't overuse your heating system in winter and the AC in summer. You can find some suggestion to keep warm with a lower impact on the environment in the newsletter of December.
  • Hang laundry instead of using the drier.
  • Switch off your electronics when you are not using them.
  • Turn the lights off when you are not in a room.

Power In the Streets: Standing for a World of Justice and Equity

Dr. King is renowned for his passion, wisdom, strategic brilliance and commitment to Non-Violence. He taught that Non-Violent Resistance is effective, but not easy. It requires us to exercise our inner-strength and resolve but can potentially be the most powerful way to grow movements, win hearts and minds to our causes and create the changes that we want to see in the world. LEGACYPOSSIBILITY
Every Child is Born a Poet 

Monday, January 18 2021, 7:00 - 8:30pm.

An incendiary mix of documentary, poetry, storytelling, drama, and performance, EVERY CHILD IS BORN A POET explores the life and work of Piri Thomas (b. 1928) the Afro-Cuban-Puerto Rican author of the classic autobiographical novel Down These Mean Streets (1967). The film traces Thomas’ path from childhood to manhood in New York City’s Spanish Harlem, El Barrio, from the 1930s through the 1960s; his parents’ immigrant experience, home life during the Great Depression, his membership in barrio youth gangs, his struggle to come to terms with his mixed-racial identity, his travels as a teen-age merchant marine, his heroin addiction, his notorious armed robbery of a Greenwich Village nightclub, his six years spent in prison, and then his emergence as a writer. Thomas’ coming-of-age story is counter-pointed with verité scenes of his on-going work of forty-five years as an educator and activist empowering marginalized and incarcerated youths. A stylized, genre-spanning production, EVERY CHILD IS BORN A POET is a riveting portrait of a life lived through struggle, self-discovery, and transformation as it examines Thomas’ use of creative expression as a means of confronting poverty, racism, violence and isolation. Directed by Jonathan Meyer Robinson, presented by The Dairy Arts Center.

GOOD NEWS OF THE WEEK

The High Ambition Coalition (HAC) for Nature and People, which is composed of the UK and countries from six other continents, has pledged to protect at least 30 percent of the Earth's land and oceans in the next 10 years. The announcement was made by President Emmanuel Macron at the One Planet summit in Paris on Monday. Scientists believe that, because of the accelerated growth of human activity and climate change, we are facing the Sixth Great Extinction on Earth. The HAC hopes their efforts at global conservation may prevent mass extinction and preserve the delicate balance of our ecosystem.
