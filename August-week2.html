TOPIC OF THE WEEK: THE MEAT PRODUCTION

In the first newsletter about the food system I tried to give a general idea of the complexity of the system and the major issues related to it. In the next few weeks I will go a bit deeper into some of the issues that I listed and I want to start today with the meat production. Why start there? As Zen master Thich Nhat Hanh has said, making a transition to a more plant-based diet may well be the most effective way an individual can stop climate change. Few climate solutions with this kind of impact lie in the hands of single individuals (and are as close as the dinner plate!). This means that our choices and our actions in this direction count a lot!

While most people still eat meat, it is increasingly common for people to switch to a meat-free, plant-based diet, or reduce their meat consumption, due to the ongoing environmental consequences of animal agriculture. Just to give an example, five million acres of rainforest are felled every year in Central & South America alone to create cattle pasture. Additionally, it takes 2,400 gallons of water and 13 pounds of grain to produce one pound of feedlot beef. This means you save more water by not eating four quarter-pound hamburgers than you do by not showering for six months! 

Why does meat production have such an impact on the environment? Haven’t humans always produced meat? Indeed, livestock production used to be an important part of small- and medium-sized independent farms; animal manure fertilized crops, animals ate farm waste products, and the sale of animal products in local markets provided important additional income to farmers. Some farms still operate this way, but this is no longer the norm, as livestock production has become big business. The modern, industrialized way in which we produce meat, eggs and other animal products has turned animals into units of production. This has left them subject to inhumane treatment, terrible living conditions and cruel deaths. In this system, to maximize efficiency and profits, companies and operators of concentrated animal feeding operations (CAFOs) prioritize rapid growth and large scale production over animal health and welfare. These conglomerates produce our food for low cost with little regard for the environment. This is especially so in terms of soil and water quality, food safety, and animal welfare. Industrial agriculture operations frequently trade the quality and safety of products, as well as the health of consumers and rural communities, for maximized profits achieved through consolidation and mechanization.

There are more sustainable ways of consuming meat, and purchasing sustainably-produced meat does reduce your personal impact. However, the rate of meat consumption that is currently the standard in many American homes is impossible to maintain with anything other than the current system. Since the current system has vast negative impacts on our environment (and many other aspects!), it is apparent that change is necessary. As it stands today, the average American eats over 3.5 lbs (1.6 kg) of meat and 15.6 lbs (7.1 kg) of cow’s milk products per week – which is several times more than in most other countries in the world. What does that mean in terms of production? Right now, the United States houses 103 million cattle (of those 10 million are milk cows), 60 million hogs and pigs, 10 million sheep and lambs, and 7 billion chickens. On top of all of that we are still importing meat to fill our annual demand.

Cramming thousands of animals in one small space separates the animals from their food source, creating a need for excessive (fossil fuel-based) transportation. It also concentrates vast amounts of putrid waste, which drains into lakes and the ocean, contributing to water pollution and ocean acidification. Additionally, because of the conditions animals are kept in, continuous antibiotic treatments are often necessary to prevent disease. According to the Food and Drug Administration, over 70% of the antibiotics produced in the US actually go to livestock! Overall, the most conservative estimates suggest that raising livestock accounts for nearly 15% of global greenhouse gases emitted each year, more comprehensive assessments say more than 50%. If cattle were their own nation, they would be the world’s third-largest emitter of greenhouse gases: Most of it comes from methane produced by ruminants, such as cows, as they digest their food. The rest comes from CO2 associated with production of animal feed, and nitrous oxide from manure and fertilizer. 

Bringing about dietary change is not simple because eating is profoundly personal and cultural. But keep in mind that plant-based sources of protein and other nutrients like calcium, iron, and omega fatty acids are so plentiful that almost all humans can thrive on a diet completely free of animal products. Plus, a reduction of meat consumption is actually a health choice for a lot of people - if this is what we choose. Plant-based alternatives are getting more and more common in restaurants and supermarkets and you can find plenty of websites with recipes for inspiration! As seen, the reasons for a more conscious consumption of meat are plentiful, going from ethical, to environmental, or health connected. You do not need to go vegan or vegetarian to have an impact: Mark Bittman (author of Food Matters) coined the term “Lessmeatarian” to best describe the spirit of moderation when consuming meat and animal products.

TIPS OF THE WEEK

Do you want to make a difference with your food choices when it comes to meat? You do not need to go completely vegan or vegetarian. Here are 8 of the most effective ways for less-destructive meat eating! If you have specific questions on this topic, write me an email! 

  1. Reduce your meat consumption. Reducing the amount of meat we eat is one of the most important steps you can take. The campaign “Meatless Monday” suggests avoiding meat once per week, on Monday. We can go a step further if we want, pushing to limit meat to one day per week: It can be the Sunday treat, the holiday delicacy. Learn how to cook every day vegetarian meals and keep the meat for special occasions.
  2. Buy from sustainable farmers. Ask questions about how the animals were raised, how the farm is operated, how much space each animal gets, whether hormones or antibiotics were used on the animals, etc. Buy directly from small farmers whenever possible. How to find them? The farmers market is the first and easiest option!
  3. Be conscious with the type of meat you buy. Purchase meat raised under standards of third-party certifiers such as Animal Welfare Approved or American Grassfed Association, or USDA Certified Organic. Make sure that the meat you buy is produced in the most sustainable possible way in all aspects. Look for beef and dairy products labeled “pasture raised” and “100 percent grassfed” or bearing a comparable designation of no supplemental grain feeding. The label “certified Organic” on pork, chicken and eggs indicates, among other practices, that all of the feed eaten by the animals was grown organically. Products bearing the Animal Welfare Approved label have met rigorous certification standards, including for feed. We will discuss more about labels and policy on food products in one of the next newsletters!
  4. Substitute red meat with poultry & fish. Red meat - beef, lamb, goat, bison, and pork – often requires more resources to produce and has comparatively severe environmental impacts. According to the Carnegie Institution for Science, eating a pound of beef causes more greenhouse gas emissions than burning a gallon of gasoline, whereas far fewer emissions are caused by the production of poultry or fish. Be careful with the source of poultry: Even if it has a lower environmental impact, chicken production has very deep ethical effects anyway.
  5. Buy unfamiliar cuts of meat and use as much of the carcass as you can, to prevent these foods from being wasted. The easiest way to do so is to purchase meat directly from the farm. In some places there are programs to buy meat only when the entire animal is sold.
  6. Hunt for wild meat. Hunting can be a more sustainable way of obtaining meat that has been in use for the entire history of humanity. Obtaining a license and tag for game is a small price to pay for the quality of the meat, the wilderness skills learned, and the connections to nature that can be obtained in the process.
  7. Raise your own! If you make the choice to eat meat, nothing is better than raising it yourself. That way, you can make sure the animals are raised respectfully, nutritiously fed, and slaughtered in the least-painful way. When you raise and prepare the animal yourself, you can take responsibility for the process, beginning to end - and you can use the entire animal. That is clearly not an option in Graduate and Family Housing, but keep it in mind for the next steps of your life!
  8. Don’t waste it. If you choose to purchase animal products, treat these foods with respect. Pouring a gallon of spoiled milk down the drain means wasting 1000 gallons of water and producing 17.6 pounds of CO2 for no reason! Keep in mind the suggestions given in the newsletter about food waste: Purchase your food in a reasonable way, store it in the best possible way and find alternatives to avoid waste.

GOOD NEWS OF THE WEEK

The U.S. is seeing incredible technological innovation in the plant-based food industry. The quality of products, and their likeness to animal-based meat, continues to astound "reducetarians" - the rise of people who are cutting back on animal products - across the country. Meat alternatives are getting so popular that most major fast food chains have a vegetarian and often a vegan option in their menu.
