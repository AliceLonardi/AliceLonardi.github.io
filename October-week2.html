QUIZ ANSWERS

The idea behind our carbon emission quiz came from a new study from the University of British Columbia that shows how most people are relatively bad in judging which environmental choices have the biggest effect on their carbon dioxide emissions. The study is based on a survey done across the USA and Canada, involving 965 people. The original survey was based on four questions: Nobody got all four questions right. The original survey had open questions, while our quiz was suggested with multiple choices. Because of that the results are not directly compared. But four out of the seven questions in our quiz are exactly the same questions of the survey (questions 1, 2, 4, and 6 are the original ones). Here are the questions and the correct answers (with the percentage of people that answered correctly).

Question 1

You decide to reduce greenhouse gas emissions by becoming a vegetarian for one year. Your friend doesn’t want to change her diet, but decides to cut down by purchasing only bulk foods (nothing with any form of packaging). How many years would it take your friend to save the same amount of greenhouse gases as you save in that year?


Answer: 11 years (10% answered correctly)

You’d have to avoid food packaging for approximately 11 years to have the same impact as one year without meat, according to a 2013 study. While the average is roughly 11 years, the possible range of correct answers is 10.5 to 11.7 years. That’s because it’s nearly impossible to completely avoid packaging and an undetermined amount of packing materials would remain in use. But, remember: This is a measure of climate impact, and while the carbon emissions of making a single plastic bag are small, plastic waste can have devastating effects on oceans, ecosystems and wildlife.

In the original survey, nearly everyone guessed low. The median guess was 1.1 years, which misses the mark by a factor of ten. In our quiz this was one of the questions with the lowest number of correct answers: Most people guessed 2.5 or 6 years. 

Question 2

Your house generates a lot of greenhouse gasses, but not all the appliances and gadgets are equal.How many hours could you leave a lamp with an LED light bulb switched on and produce the same amount of greenhouse gases as a single load in a clothes dryer?


Answer: 300 hours (20% answered correctly)

You could leave an LED light on for roughly 300 hours, or 13 days straight, and have the same carbon footprint as one load in the dryer. (The correct answer range is 272 to 354 hours, depending on the brightness of the bulb and on the dryer.) 

In the original survey group, the median guess was 60 hours. In our quiz most people answered 60 hours as well (40% of the answers). 

Question 3

You need to buy new running shoes. You find a pair you like in a local store and you decide to buy them. You also decide to make up for the greenhouse emissions of those shoes by giving up the next TV series. How many hours of watching TV should you skip to offset the new shoes? 

Answer: 150 hours (40% answered correctly)

You would have to keep your TV on for 150 hours, or 6.25 days straight, to have the same carbon footprint as needed to produce a typical pair of running shoes. Different types of shoes have a different footprint: The emission for textile shoes are about a third of those of running shoes, while leather shoes have an impact four times higher. In all cases, most of those emissions come from the manufacturing process, especially as most shoes are made in China where coal is the dominant source of electricity. The notable exception is leather shoes, where the biggest impact is due to cattle rearing. 

In our quiz, this was one of the most correctly answered questions: 40% of you got it right!

Question 4

Say you’re taking a one-way, economy flight from New York to London. You might want to make up for those emissions by giving up quarter-pound hamburgers. How many burgers would you need to skip to offset that flight? 

Answer: 278 burgers (40% answered correctly)             

You’d need to skip approximately 278 burgers. The range of correct answers was between 146 and 410. That’s because different studies have different estimates of the emissions that come from raising cattle and processing meat. That’s equivalent to all the beef consumed by the average American in 15 months. So, if you don’t plan to stay in London and you don’t want to take a sailboat home, you’d have to give up beef for more than two years in order to offset the emissions from your round trip. 

In the original survey group, the median guess was 100 burgers. It seems that in our case the awareness for the impact that flights have on the environment is higher: Most people guessed correctly and the second most popular answer was 472, way above the median guess in the original survey. 

Question 5

Say you’re taking the same one-way, economy flight from New York to London. You might want to make up for those emissions by reducing your showering time from 10 to 5 minutes per daily shower. How many months would you need to reduce your showering time to offset that flight?

Answer: 20 months (10% answered correctly)

You’d need to reduce your showering times to 5 minutes every day for 20 months. It means that in order to offset your round trip you’d need to half your showering times for more than 3 years. 

Interestingly enough, most of you guessed a higher number of months, somehow underestimating the impact of your showering routine. Keep also in mind that in this calculation what is considered is the energy needed to warm up the water you use. The impact you have in cutting down showering time is also in water usage, which in a place like Colorado might be very significant!

Question 6

You have a midsize car and you’re environmentally conscious, so you set yourself a strict limit of 100 miles per week. If you switched to a hybrid car, how many miles could you drive while still producing the same amount of greenhouse gases?                 

Answer: 150 miles (20% answered correctly)                         

On average, you could drive about 151 miles, but it depends on the cars. For example, if you switch from a Toyota Camry to a Toyota Prius, you could drive 190 miles — almost twice as far! The correct range is 112 to 190 miles, because different cars have different performance.

The study group tended to overestimate the carbon trade-off, with a median guess of 200 miles. For our quiz the most guessed answer was 250.

Question 7

You decide to reduce greenhouse gas emissions by eating vegan once per week ("Vegan Monday") for a year. Your friend doesn’t want to change her diet, but decides to cut down by biking to work every Monday. She lives 2.5 km (1.6 miles) from her work place and she is driving an SUV. How many years would it take your friend to save the same amount of greenhouse gases as you save in that year?

Answer: 1 years (20% answered correctly)

Biking to work one day per week has more or less the same impact as cutting in animal products one day per week! This comparison is valid only if you live relatively close to your workplace, as it is for us living in Graduate and Family Housing: If you live further away, driving less might have a bigger impact. Bike to work can be replaced with using public transportation to work: The emissions per person on a bus, especially at rush hour, are minimal compared to commuting by car alone. 

As I said last week, this quiz was very difficult. The highest number of correct answers was 3/7. And this is because those calculations are extremely difficult to make, and for some specific activities/habits different studies give different carbon emission values. But having a feeling for what has the biggest impact on the environment can help us, especially in focusing our effort in meaningful actions. When people in the survey were asked to pick the most significant lifestyle change they could make to cut emissions, driving less was the most popular answer. Indeed, the three most meaningful changes are living car-free, avoiding air travel, and eating a vegetarian diet. (Eating vegetarian, for example, is eight times more effective than upgrading light bulbs.) This does not want to imply that “smaller” actions, like reducing the amount of waste, do not have an impact: As said before, those evaluations deal with the carbon footprint, while other environmental impacts, like on biodiversity, ecosystems, wildlife, and health issues are ignored. 

The United States and Canada are two of the top greenhouse gas emitters worldwide, both in total and per capita emissions. On average, each person produces over 15 tons of carbon dioxide each year. That’s around three times as much as the British average and about eight times as much as in India. Fighting climate change needs the involvement of everybody, from policy changes on a government level, to emission reduction on the industry level. Individual carbon footprints, particularly in the world’s wealthiest countries, add up. Household emissions, which include energy use, food purchases and transportation, make up a fifth of all emissions in the United States.


Curious about the topic and interested in additional data or inputs? Here are some resources you might find useful:

  • The EPA is the best source of data about carbon emissions in the USA. You can find some easy to read fast facts about emissions between 1990 and 2018, and a more complete data highlight. If you would like to have the same type of data for other countries, an amazing resource is the greenhouse gas emission page of ourworldindata.org: You can select the country you want and even compare the emission of several countries!
  • If you want to dig deeper in the complicated world of carbon emission evaluation, a good starting point is this article “Uncovering the Carbon Footprint of Everything”: It give a nice review of the book “How Bad are Bananas?: The Carbon Footprint of Everything” by Mike Berners-Lee and it includes data on things or actions we didn’t consider at all (like for example texting, e-mailing or googling).
  • Want to talk about carbon emission with your kids? This from gokid might be a good starting point.

TIPS OF  THE WEEK

 

Interested in learning more about your carbon footprint and reducing your emissions? Take a look at this suggestions:

  1. Take a carbon footprint test. The most thorough and engaging calculators are those by Carbon Footprint Ltd., Carbon Independent, and Cool Climate Network. (This paper gives a detailed comparison and review of a lot of different calculators available).
  2. Identify the areas where you produce more emissions. 
  3. Make a plan to reduce. Simple actions like biking to work or reducing meat in your diet have a big impact but they don’t impact your daily routine. If something is just not an option for you, find other actions that can offset the behaviors you can’t change. Be real with that: Use the carbon footprint test and the quiz suggested to train your feeling on that! Take also a look at this article: the most effective five ways to reduce your carbon footprint are listed with a very clear explaination.

Sandor Katz, "Fermentation as Metaphor"

October 20 at 5 PM

Author Sandor Katz will speak about his new book, Fermentation as Metaphor, in this virtual event. In 2012, Sandor Ellix Katz published The Art of Fermentation, which quickly became the bible for foodies around the world, a runaway bestseller, and a James Beard Book Award winner. In Fermentation as Metaphor, stemming from his personal obsession with all things fermented, Katz meditates on his art and work, drawing connections between microbial communities and aspects of human culture: politics, religion, social and cultural movements, art, music, sexuality, identity, and even our individual thoughts and feelings. He informs his arguments with his vast knowledge of the fermentation process, which he describes as a slow, gentle, steady, yet unstoppable force for change. Fermentation as Metaphor broadens and redefines our relationship with food and fermentation. It's the perfect gift for serious foodies, fans of fermentation, and non-fiction readers alike.
