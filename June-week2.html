TOPIC OF THE WEEK: REDUCE

Last week our journey into trash started from an unusual point of view: Rethinking the system. The focus shifted from finding a solution to dispose of our trash back to the source of the problem, the production of trash itself.

Avoiding purchasing is an amazing way to avoid having to dispose of this stuff. This starts from refusing little useless items that find their way into our life almost without us being aware of them. I am thinking of junk mail, for example. Most of the time junk mail like coupons and advertisements end up directly in the trash, we don’t even look at it. And what about the receipts that we ask to get printed when we purchase something? Do we use them, or do they end up directly in the trash? The amount and the variety of this type of “free” goodies is extensive. Being aware of them and saying a simple no to them is a small change in our life, but one that has a real impact.

Rethinking what we really need and avoiding what we buy just because of induced needs is the first necessary step to reduce the amount of things we throw away. But what about the things that we really need? Luckily we have plenty of alternatives to buying new objects!

  • How about sharing? Before buying higher-end items that you don’t plan on using every day, can you borrow it from a neighbor or friend? Consider shared purchases with your friends: this helps the environment and your wallet! It also increases your social life, giving you opportunities to get in contact with people.
    The sharing economy is upon us with online tools popping up all over to help neighbors and even strangers to share, rent, and borrow. And I am not just thinking about bike sharing or car sharing, that are getting more and more common. You can find plenty of options for shared tools or materials, and the classic book-sharing system, libraries! Those are amazing ways to avoid buying things that we might need, but we can happily avoid to own.

Did you know that in Boulder we have a Tool Rental Library, where you can access more than 300 hand, electric, and gas-powered tools for all your crafty projects for a very affordable price? Did you know that the same is valid for tons of reclaimed materials that you can find at the Resource Central? Additionally we have the Household Hazardous Waste Facility that has a free reuse shop where you can find auto and hobby products, garden and yard products, home maintenance and cleaning products, paint and more. Another amazing resource for crafty projects is Art Parts Boulder, a nonprofit that inspires and promotes creativity, resource conservation, and community engagement through reuse.
If you need books, movies or magazines, visit the local library. Boulder Public Library is an amazing resource! And have you checked all the little free libraries that you can find all around the city?

  • How about repairing? Extend the lifetime of your favorite things by taking time to repair them. This helps keeping perfectly fine items out of the land fill. If it's worth saving, why not saving it?! This is valid for bikes, clothes, jewelry, electronics, furniture, kitchen items… Basically everything that you own! And the best part of it is that trying to fix your broken things does not cost you anything. They are already broken, so it can just get better!

Check out I Fix It and iFixit for free repair manuals for thousands of items. They even review items based on their ability to be easily repaired, so you can make more informed purchases next time you need to buy something new. The same is valid for the Repair Clinic. Take a look at the Fixit Clinic blogspot or attend one of the free community Repair Cafes to help people get in touch with the value of their consumer goods. Use the U-Fix-It Clinics happening every two months at the Boulder Public Library. Take a look at their Facebook page for updates. There are several shops in Boulder that repair phones and other electronics. Look for them when you don’t feel confident fixing things yourself.

  • How about swapping or buying second hand? Before you head to the store to buy something new, check thrift shops and online exchange sites. There are things we can’t avoid buying, but the amount of resources that we can save by buying them second hand or exchanging them makes this a great alternative.

Craigslist is the biggest, fastest moving exchange site. You can find anything, from expensive cars to free shelves. FreeCycle is a network of local people who post and trade free unwanted items via a Yahoo groups list. An online trading site for finding and selling used building materials by zip code is BuildingSurplus. The site also features home improvement tips, forums for exchanging ideas and news about recycled and reused building supplies. Find a list of second hand stores in Boulder on this page. There are a lot more, this is far from being exhaustive!

  • How about thinking of new ways? The previous points list already existing possibilities. But our imagination does not have limits! TIPS OF THE WEEK

The possibilities to reduce the amount of trash that we produce in a week are endless. Try to focus on one of those options this week and maybe go to the next ones in the following weeks.

  1. Try to be aware of some “unwanted” items. What kinds of items accumulate in your home that you never reuse?
  2. Opt out of the junk mail. Follow the directions suggested by the Eco-Cycle here. It does not have an immediate effect, but you will start to get less junk mail in a couple of weeks!
  3. Refuse freebies at events, promotional stands, shop openings and such. Keep this in mind when these situations will restart happening!
  4. Go paperless for a day this week! Can your journal be digital? How about taking notes or reading papers/magazines digitally?
  5. Switch to electronic bill notifications and payment. Choose digital receipts when you buy something.
  6. Rent or borrow one item you were going to buy this week.
  7. Repair an item instead of purchasing a new one.

GOOD NEWS OF THE WEEK

With people all across the U.S. spending more time at home during coronavirus quarantines, many Americans are rethinking their habits and trying to live more sustainably. A survey of 2,000 people found that nearly two-thirds of Americans have been inspired to pick up more eco-friendly habits during the pandemic and beyond. Specifically, 64 percent of respondents have had an "eco wake-up call," inspiring them to make various changes to their habits - including recycling more (55 percent), using less paper products (43 percent) and reducing food waste (44 percent).
