TOPIC OF THE WEEK: REDUCE 2.0

How to go  Zero Waste and reduce the amount of trash we produce is a complex issue. The system we live in relies on production and consumption of things, and by consequence, disposing of those things. In the last weeks we started to RETHINK the system, focusing on REFUSING useless items that sneak into our lives and finding ALTERNATIVES to buying new items. Sharing, swapping, repairing, buying second hand are some of the examples I suggested last week. What is the next piece of the puzzle? 

Googling for “Zero Waste” might be very overwhelming: You can find videos like this videos like this one, explaining how a family of 4 people is able to produce one jar of waste per year. This can certainly be inspiring. But it can also feel like an unreachable goal. Let’s focus on the things you can start with if you want to take on the challenge!


The first step is always to become aware of the problem. Ask yourself: How many things are you throwing away every day, what are those things and are they things that you used multiple times or are they items that you used only once? Most of the stuff we throw away are items that we use every day and that we can’t share or exchange, but a lot can be done by preferencing recyclable containers when choosing what product to buy, and reusing containers whenever possible. 

Because of this, the next question is how much of this you can recycle or compost. I am going to give you more details about recycling and composting in the next newsletters, but just as a quick preview: Not all materials have the same impact on the planet! Recycling works incredibly well for some of them, but it is clearly not the solution for others. The biggest problem we have is plastic. If you are interested in discovering more about this, take a look at the resources at the end and stay tuned for the next newsletter. 

For now, keep these quick notes in mind:

  1. Plastic has a huge production impact. When you can, seek alternatives! 
  2. Plastic is designed to last long, so getting rid of it is not easy. 
  3. In some cases, plastic is not recyclable, for either technical or economical reasons. 
  4. The plastics that are actually recycled lose quality and value and eventually end up in the landfill. Glass and Aluminum can be recycled infinitely! When you can, look for items in these materials!
  5. It was estimated that only 10% of the plastic that we ever produced got actually ever recycled.

Here are three general concepts you need to guide your shift to Zero Waste: 

  1. Can you replace an item you throw away with something you can REUSE? 
  2. Can you replace a plastic item with another one made of a different material? 
  3. Can you do that with the stuff you dispose of the most?

In this next step of our journey, identify the stuff you dispose of the most and consider alternatives. It takes a bit of effort to remember to take your reusable item with you or to look for plastic free alternatives at the shops, but be persistent! You can find 7 suggestions (one per day) of common items and their reusable/plastic free alternative in the “tips of the week” part, together with some tricks to help with the switch and some reasons why it is important. Slowly implement steps at your own pace. Check what works the best for you and have fun discovering new ways of using stuff!

TIPS OF THE WEEK

The possibilities to reduce the amount of trash that we produce in a week are endless. Try to focus on one of those options this week and maybe go to the next ones in the following weeks. If you want to do it all in the next 7 days then, fantastic, the planet loves overachievers!

1.Shop with a reusable bag. Every year, Americans reportedly throw away 100 billion plastic grocery bags (The Worldwatch Institute) and of the plastic bags used less than 5% are recycled (E.P.A.). This is the easiest change to implement, even now with the COVID-19 crisis -  bring your bag with you and fill it yourself! You can wash it with normal dish soap at home after using it if you want to be extra careful. It is a small step for you, but a huge one for the environment!  In Boulder, stores even credit you for bringing your bag (10 cents per bag, it adds up)! Think about the cabinet space you’ll save by no longer needing that plastic sack stash area! 


Want some tips to remember to take a bag with you? 

  • Keep some in your car, purse, or backpack
  • Hang them on the door so that you see them when you leave 
  • Carry your groceries home without a bag if you don’t have reusable bags

Don’t fret if you still forget them from time to time. As said before, habits take time to form. Want to get a reusable bag for free? It’s as easy as writing an email to the sustainability liaison! Or if you are really creative, take a look at the video that one of the Apartment Assistants (previously called Resident Managers) shared!

2. Use a reusable water bottle. As a nation, we throw away 35 billion plastic bottles a year. Ditching bottled water for a reusable water bottle not only saves energy and resources but will also save you money, as bottled water is typically sold for 10,000 times the price of regular tap water. The water in Boulder is absolutely safe and constantly checked. If you do not like the taste of tap, opt for a filter for your faucet so that you get the taste that you enjoy. The filters are usually recyclable through their manufacturer and this still saves many plastic bottles from going into circulation. It might take a while to get into the habit of always having a water bottle on us, but the dry climate in Boulder is a very good incentive. On campus you can find refill stations everywhere and also in the city water stations are easy to find.

3. Ditch Paper Towels. Did you know that “every day, over 3,000 tons of paper towel waste is produced in the US alone?” Paper is produced from trees, and cutting down trees to produce something that you only use once feels like a waste (literally!) of resources. This is a good opportunity to upcycle socks or any other piece of clothing that can’t be saved. Cut the fabric into squares, put them in a kitchen drawer, or in a basket under the sink. If you want to get fancy, check out some of those ideas for “do it yourself” swiffer duster, swiffer duster pads or rags. When I need to clean the house or wipe up a mess, I use reusable rags that I just throw into the laundry when the rag is dirty. Don’t want to mix your clothes with stuff you use for cleaning? Keep a dedicated laundry basket for towels and rags and wash them separately. Such an easy swap that will eliminate more of your trash and save you more money!

4. Reusable Coffee Cup. We drink a lot of coffee. 587 million cups per day for Americans. That is almost 2 per person per day! That’s a lot of cups that are going right into the garbage. And normal disposable coffee cups are not recyclable. Instead of getting that coffee in a disposable cup, bring your own! Carrying a reusable mug can get you discounts at coffee shops on campus and throughout Boulder. I know, this is not an easy switch in this situation. Most coffee shops at the moment are not accepting reusable mugs. Keep this trick in mind for the end of the pandemic and for now choose your coffee shop paying attention: There are compostable alternatives to the traditional coffee cups and quite some shops in Boulder use those!

5. Use cloth produce bags. Smaller cloth bags can serve multiple purposes at a grocery store. Use them for produce, bulk products, bread, pastries, etc. The produce department in our grocery store encourages the use of tiny, flimsy plastic sacks. Do we really need them? For most of our fruits and veggies, probably not. However, there are cases where we like to buy a pound of green beans or more than one tomato. Or we prefer our food to avoid contact with all the surfaces at the cash station. The solution? Get yourself some cloth produce bags. These are just smaller, cinch bags that you use in replace of the plastic sack. You can easily find them in most supermarkets (check in the bulk section) or you can get creative and make them yourself. The bag weight is normally not a problem, but if the ones you use are a little heavier, write the weight onto the cloth bag and have the cashier take the difference off the total weight amount. Worried about conserving the produce at home without a plastic bag? Take a look at the suggestions given on this webpage:

6. Bring Your Own Utensils. Plastic cutlery is obnoxious. It’s flimsy, very wasteful, and used everywhere. It’s estimated that 40 billion plastic utensils are thrown away every year in the United States and this is usually from one use. Avoiding this disposable is very simple. Keep your own reusable utensils with you. Keep a fork with you at work, stick one in your purse, or stick one in your car. You can get a set of bamboo utensils to keep in your purse at all times for free from the sustainability liaison! They are light, solid and fancy, and when they get too old or damaged you can compost them. Write to gfhsustainability@colorado.edu if you still don’t have one.

7. Compostable Toothbrush. The normal suggestion with toothbrushes is to replace them every 3-4 months. Do you know what that means? About 50 million pounds of toothbrushes get sent to the landfill each year. Crazy, right? A plastic toothbrush is just another disposable that we have been told that we need to use per marketing. This is another easy swap. When your current plastic toothbrush is just too worn out for teeth brushing, add it to your cleaning supplies, and get yourself a sustainable toothbrush like one made from bamboo! They work just as effectively as their wasteful, plastic counterparts and can be composted when they wear out. You can find compostable toothbrushes at Natural Grocers, EarthHero, RefillRevolution, Sprouts, Whole Food, Walgreens, and many others.
