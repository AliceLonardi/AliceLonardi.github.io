TOPIC OF THE WEEK: COMPOSTING

After six weeks of information about how to handle the trash problem we are all experts in handling our garbage. The only type of waste we have not talked about yet is organic waste, which is food scraps or yard waste. What is the difference with the rest of the stuff we throw away?

The common idea is that organic waste is “natural” stuff that breaks down in a “natural” way: a leaf falling from a tree will rot and transform into nutrients for the soil, eventually helping new plants grow. When buried in a landfill, that same leaf - or those lettuce heads or grass clippings you have laying around - doesn't break down as it would in nature. In a landfill, they decompose anaerobically, (without oxygen) and in the process become the number one source of human-caused methane - a major player in climate change. Methane is a powerful greenhouse gas: It traps 84 times more heat in the atmosphere than carbon dioxide over the short term. The impact of methane from organic waste on climate change is massive: nationwide, 50% of what goes into landfills is organic material!

What is the solution there? COMPOSTING! When putting your organic waste in the compost bins, you send it to a composting facility, where it gets combined and it breaks down aerobically, with oxygen, using the natural process of decomposition. In this way we make use of nature’s ultimate recycling system and create a rich soil-amendment called compost. 

Compost is a dark, crumbly, and an earthy-smelling form of decomposed organic matter. It can be used to restore our farmland and promote sustainable agriculture. We will talk more about food production and its environmental impact in the next newsletter. For this week we focus on the fact that compost enriches soil and helps it retain nutrients that produce healthier, more nutrient-dense food. With a natural increase of food productivity, there is less need for fertilizers and pesticides. Additionally, compost helps soil retain water by increasing its absorbency! This also reduces water use in the fields! Another significant impact of compost is that by applying it to soils, we create a carbon “sink” by increasing the soil’s ability to pull carbon from the atmosphere and store it safely in the ground. Soil has the capacity to store three times more carbon than trees! Composting is an incredibly powerful climate-change-fighting tool: Not only does it avoid the production of methane, but it also helps reduce carbon dioxide in the atmosphere!

What can you put in the compost dumpsters?

  • food scraps, any type: if you can eat it, you can compost it!;
  • dirty paper and cardboard: greasy pizza boxes that you cannot recycle, paper plates or take-out food boxes (only if they are not plastic-coated!!), paper tissues, paper napkins, coffee filters; 
  • any type of certified compostable products: they need to have the compostable product label or have “compostable” written on them. Any doubts? Take a look at this page to learn how to identify compostable products.

TIPS OF THE WEEK

Do you want to start composting? Here are some practical suggestions to make it easy!

  1. Keep a small composting bucket in your kitchen to collect food scraps. The freezer is a great spot to use to prevent smells or fruit flies. You can contact me at gfhsustainability@colorado.edu and ask for one for free!
  2. If you don't want to clean your bin regularly, then line the bucket with a compostable bag. The bag can easily be tied closed and disposed of in the compost dumpster and it will break down just as well as the goods inside! Be careful: don’t use plastic bags but compostable bags!
  3. If you don’t want to buy compostable bags, use a paper bag or paper towel on the bottom of the bin. It helps absorb the liquids produced by the decomposition. 
    Want to avoid fruit flies in summer? Empty your compost bin and rinse it regularly. Alternatively, keep the bin in the freezer! It is safe (no contamination of your food) and you can avoid any mushy stuff in the bin. 
  4. What if you start getting fruit flies? Make a trap! Take a container and put a small quantity of vinegar. Add a couple of drops of dish soap and leave it in your kitchen overnight. The fruit flies are attracted by the vinegar smell but the soap breaks the surface tension of the vinegar so the fruit flies can't just sit atop the liquid and they will sink and drown.
  5. Plastic in the compost means micro plastic in our fields and in our food: Avoid plastic in the compost bin at any cost! Learn how to distinguish compostable items from plastic-coated ones, get familiar with labels. Take a look at this page to get more information. If you are not sure if your disposable is plastic coated or not, throw it in the landfill.

ACTIVITIES

Want to play with composting? Here are some ideas of activities to do with your kids or to experiment yourself!

  • Composting Scavenger Hunt for younger kids. Composting is a process that is happening all around you! Help your kids get familiar with decomposition and natural composting. Take some time to explore around your yard or neighborhood for signs of composting with them. Suggest to keep a scientists journal to write or draw their discoveries. Here are some suggested questions to start the observations: 1. Look for three things that can be composted. What did you find? 2. Try to find a roly-poly (but don’t touch! It is hard at work making healthy soils!) Draw the roly-poly and where you found it. 3. Look for roots. Draw the roots and the plant. 4. Look for plants or leaves in the process of decomposition, and sketch them. 5. Look for earthworms. Draw them below, and show where you found them. 6. What else did you see? 
  • Experiment with composting at home (ages 8 and up). Here is a suggestion for an easy 2-3 weeks experiment to do in your kitchen with just a plastic bottle, food scraps, some leaves and a bit of soil.

GOOD NEWS OF THE WEEK

Carbon farming has been proven to increase soils’ ability to sequester carbon dioxide on farm lands. In 2019, Eco-Cycle expanded that concept by conducting carbon farming trials for the first time on urban landscapes – lawns, gardens, parks, and medians. The Community Carbon Farming Campaign, a three-year trial of carbon farming techniques on urban lands, is engaging more than 250 volunteers as “Carbon Farmers” who are using their lawns and gardens to test different soil amendments, and from which Eco-Cycle drew soil samples that were analyzed in a lab. The project is entering the second year and it is expanding with the participation of new volunteers as Observational Carbon Farmers. You can find out more about the project, including the results from the first year.
