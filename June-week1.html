TOPIC OF THE WEEK: RETHINK

I want to start our journey together talking about trash. Why? 


Trash is a topic that connects almost all issues related to sustainability: Usage of resources, greenhouse emissions and climate change, pollution, habitat disruption, social justice, and many others all tie back to waste. All of us are getting more and more aware of the issue, and we’ve all probably seen the disturbing pictures of our seas or beaches full of garbage, or marine life trapped in some sort of trash. But how is this problem affecting us? And what can we do about it? 

 

The common solution that is normally suggested to resolve the trash problem is RECYCLING. We’ll definitely talk about that in a lot of future newsletters, however today I want to start our conversation with a different perspective: What if recycling alone isn’t the answer? What if there isn’t ONE simple solution, and this is a complex issue involving our lifestyle, our society, and our culture in a deeper way? 


99% of everything that gets produced ends up in the trash in less than 6 months - that means most of what we buy is literally designed to be disposed of! According to the Environmental Protection Agency, Americans generate about 268 million tons of trash annually—about 4.5 lbs/2 kg per person per day. Other countries have similar statistics. Should we start to consider that a system that produces this amount of trash is the REAL problem? What if instead of trying to manage our waste more effectively, we stopped producing it in the first place?


In contrast, nature functions on sustainable cycles, where all discarded materials are designed to become resources for others to use and nothing is wasted. Everything composts or recycles itself naturally - everything is reused, and everything biodegrades. What if we designed our system this way? 

This week, consider this: It is necessary to start to rethink our system, to “invent” new policies and practices to emulate those sustainable natural cycles. A shift from a linear system to a circular one. Some companies have started to implement “circular” economic practices into their production. What ways do we have to do the same at home?

ZERO WASTE lifestyle denotes actions focused on trash that try to achieve this shift. It involves 7 steps, and those are going to be the first steps of our journey together. The first is RETHINK.

TIP OF THE WEEK

Go deeper in discovering more about ZERO WASTE and circular economy. After watching some of the suggested videos, think what small or big changes you could implement in your daily life.

  1. Think about items you easily throw away, and try to determine whether you could replace them with reusable alternatives or avoid them altogether.
  2. What is “perceived obsolescence”? What items have you owned that you felt needed replacing, but were still performing well?
  3. In the U.S., most of our leisure time is used to watch TV and shop. When you think about the leisure time activities which bring you the most joy and rejuvenation, what are they?
  4. Why doesn’t recycling get to the core of the problem?
  5. What are some of the steps that we in the U.S. can take to reduce the quantity and impact of our nation’s consumption? What about your own country?
  6. What options do you have to change the system to support sustainability and justice?
