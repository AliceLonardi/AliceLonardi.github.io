Let’s  keep in mind that collective changes are not possible without conscious individuals that push for those changes!

TOPIC OF THE WEEK: HEATING

Many more activities are related to our carbon footprint than we generally think about. For every daily activity that you do, there is a more eco-friendly way to do it, a less eco-friendly way to do it, and lots of levels in between. What you can do is make the choice that works for the environment, and then have that choice become the norm in your lifestyle. Reduce your carbon footprint and encourage others to do the same. In this newsletter series, we are going to check some simple ways to reduce emissions in your daily life. And seeing all the snow still outside since last week, we are going to start with lowering our carbon footprint keeping our apartment warm!

Heating is the largest energy expense in most American homes, accounting for 35-50 percent of annual energy bills in colder parts of the country. On space heating alone, Americans spend $73 billion each year. Even if heating is not as energy consuming as air conditioning (we will talk about that in spring!), its impact is not negligible. Obviously, there are big differences depending on the heating system in place, but most of the furnaces or boilers in buildings, run on fossil fuels such as natural gas and oil; as a result, heating and hot water can account for up to 42 percent of a city’s total greenhouse gas emissions. Living in Graduate and Family Housing the heating costs are hidden, as they are included in your rent. Even if being conscious of how we keep our apartment warm during the winter does not have a direct immediate effect on our wallet, that does not mean that the impact on the environment is zero!

Without going too much into details, heating systems have four basic components: 

  • The heat source: Normally a furnace, this is the part that provides warm air or water;
  • The heat distribution: Commonly a radiator or forced air, this part moves the warm air or water through the house;
  • The control system: A thermostat that allows you to regulate the heat level;
  • The house insulation: Often we don’t consider the house insulation as part of the heating system, but this is what allows us to keep the heat inside longer! 

Generally, increasing the efficiency of those components allows us to keep our apartments warm and lower our carbon footprint. You can find more details in this easy infographic from the Energy Department.

Unfortunately, in Graduate and Family Housing we don’t have control over all the heating system components (in some cases we can’t control any of them!). Additionally, the buildings are relatively old and inefficient: If you live in Athens or Marine Court, you are probably unable to lower the heat and your apartment is normally too hot, while if you live in Smiley Court most probably you need an extra layer to feel comfortable. Even in these cases we still can follow easy suggestions, that can help us to feel more comfortable at home and at the same time have an impact on our carbon footprint!

The first suggestion is contact me and let me know how the situation is in your apartment! I am very happy to give personal tips and help with easy tricks! Additionally, the following are some of the general easy-to-do and inexpensive techniques. The most complicated will take a weekend afternoon, and many take little time and don't even require the purchase of materials, only changing a habit or two. Others can be done for as little as $10. Keep in mind that each degree you lower the thermostat on your heating system decreases your fuel bill (and your emissions!) by 3 percent. Going from 72 degrees down to 68 degrees doesn't matter much in terms of comfort, but it can save up to 12 percent on your heating bill.

Work on your efficiency!

  • Take a look at the specific heating system in your area. 
  • If you don’t have complete control over the heaters (complete switch on and off), call maintenance and ask for a check. 
  • Try to keep the thermostat to 69-70 F (level 3-4) and use the keep warm suggestions you can find below.
  • Keep heaters and furnaces free from furniture, toys or clothes.
  • Do not close the supply grills or vents in your apartment.
  • Keep windows and doors closed when the heating is on.
  • Keep empty rooms or rooms that you use less closed.
  • If you leave the apartment for more than 2 days, keep the heating at the minimum (thermostat at 65 F, level 1-2) but don’t switch it off completely.

Keep warm!

  • Open all windows at once, once or twice per day, while the heating is off, for 5-10 minutes for air circulation and keep them closed otherwise.
  • Open curtains and blinders when it is sunny and close them in the evening: Get the warm in, keep the cold out!
  • Keep the bathroom door open after showering and avoid fans.
  • Keep the oven open after using it (once it is off!).
  • Wear warm clothes: Thick sweaters, warm socks, unicorn jumper, …
  • Drink warm teas or infuses and eat hot soups: They are healthy and delicious and they are perfect for the winter!
  • Keep a warm blanket on the sofa for movie nights, reading books, studying or working.
  • Use extra warm blankets on the bed, warm pajamas, hot water bottles to warm up your bed.
  • Prefer small rooms that you can close for more static activities like reading, working on your laptop, watching movies.

Work on your insulation

  • Consider getting your windows sealed, especially in the bedroom: You can request it using Fixit.
  • If you don’t want a complete seal, you can consider a self-stick rubber foam weatherseal that you can apply at your window to make them more insulating. This is an example of those, you can find them in every hardware store in Boulder!
  • Consider installing thermal curtains: They work super well for the temperature and they help with light and privacy.
  • Draft stoppers are fun and keep the cold air out. You can even make one yourself using only a long thin cloth sack, like a bean bag and filling it with dried peas or rice, something to make it heavy enough to stay in place.
  • Consider a carpet or a blanket on the ground for extra insulation: This is great for the areas where the kids are playing or under your desk if you are working long hours in front of a computer!
