TOPIC OF THE WEEK: RECYCLE

In the last four weeks we had a lot of “trash talks”! The problem with waste is a very complex one and the approach to solve it must happen on several levels. I hope I managed to show you new ways to REDUCE the amount at the source and the importance of REUSING things instead of disposing of them. Those steps are fundamental to solve the problem. Today the focus is on the last step of the waste hierarchy: RECYCLE!

Why is recycling so important? All things produced are made of natural resources that come from the planet: Metal and glass from the extraction of materials from mines, paper and cardboard from trees, plastic from oil and gas. These resources are limited. If everyone consumed resources like we do here in the U.S., we would need 5 planets' worth of resources to keep up! Clearly that math doesn't add up, our consumption is not sustainable. To maintain the value of these precious resources we need to keep them in the production system as long as possible by recycling as much as we can when we decide to dispose of products. It makes sense to use and reuse materials over and over rather than putting them in the landfill and starting the whole process over again!

Unfortunately, recycling isn’t available everywhere and what can be recycled strictly depends on the program of the place you live in. In particular, it is all based on the sorting system in place and on the end market for the materials that are recycled (who is buying materials, and what they can do with them). In Boulder we are lucky, and we work with. Eco-Cycle - an amazing organization in the community. But when you move away, you will need to look up the guidelines of the new place you live, and they can be very different from what you are learning now! 

What can you recycle in Graduate and Family Housing?

Boulder has a single stream recycling system, which means you can put everything in the same recycling bin. In Graduate and Family Housing we have one additional separation for cardboard. Here is a very quick overview on what you can put in the recycling bins:

  • Paper and cardboard: Anything  bigger than 2”x2” (post-it size) can be recycled. If you have smaller pieces of paper, very colorful ones,  greasy paper (like paper towels or napkins), or cardboard (like a pizza box) put them in the compost bin! Clean and flattened cardboard has dedicated bins (the big ones with the side opening), because they take up a lot of room, but be sure to recycle them too!
  • Metal and glass containers: All of them are recyclable! Separate the lids if they are of a different material and avoid broken glass. Do not flatten them and quickly rinse them before disposing of them! You can also ball up your aluminum foil to a 2”x2” ball to recycle it!
  • Plastic: Only containers can be recycled. Forget about the recycling symbol and focus only on the shape! Bottles, jar, jugs and boxes can all be recycled if you don’t flatten them and they don’t have food leftovers. Like for metal and glass containers, leave the lids on only if they are of the same material (plastic on plastic, metal on metal).
  • Milk and juice boxes are the only mixed material items that can be recycled! 

Want more details? The city of Boulder helps us with that. They provide a really wonderful video tutorial here. If you are not sure what to do with a specific item, you can look it up on the Eco-Cycle website or send me a quick email at gfhsustainability@colorado.edu (if you include a picture of the item you are not sure about, it would make things easier!). 


I want to say this again, because it is a very important point: We have a problem with plastic, and recycling is not the solution. The recycled plastic market is very limited and has several issues - but when we do use disposables, it’s better than a landfill.

I recommended some documentaries last week, and I’ll list them again here:

Both of these give a complete and deep insight on recycling and waste. We need to limit the amount of plastic we use as much as we can! But for metals, glass and paper/cardboard recycling works very well and it is a fantastic way to reduce the use of energy and resources!

TIPS OF THE WEEK

This is the second week of suggestions to switch from disposable items to reusable or plastic free alternatives. Again, try to focus on one of those options this week and maybe go to the next ones in the following weeks. If you want to do it all in the next 7 days then, fantastic, the planet loves overachievers!

1. Buy Bulk. The bulk section is such a great ally to go Zero Waste that I decided to mention it - even if most supermarkets have closed bulk sections during the pandemic. Please, keep it in mind and experiment with it as soon as it’s back! In most supermarkets in Boulder you can find package free products that you can get using your own bags or containers. The bulk sections usually consist of a few aisles of items like flour, sugar, spices, nuts, cereals, grains, and candy that are completely package-free. A lot of stores even have bulk peanut butter, honey, oils, and we even have a dedicated store dedicated store for soaps, shampoos, laundry detergents, and cleaners.

2. Shop at the Bakery. Most local bakeries, farmers markets and even supermarkets have sections with freshly baked goods that are yet to be packaged. By heading along with your own reusable bread bag or containers, you can easily avoid single-use plastic packaging. If you forget to bring your own, you can also ask for a paper bag (which you can recycle later on if clean, or compost if it is dirty). Do you want to go pro? Buy flour from the bulk section (when it’s open again) and bake your own bread! 

3. Ditch Paper Tissues. The average American uses two boxes of disposable tissues per month. Can you imagine the saving of money and resources? To avoid disposable tissues, use a hankie or make your own homemade tissues by cutting up an old shirt or sheet and putting them into a container. Once the tissues or hankies have been used, throw them in with your normal wash. You can keep them in a jar or any other container and even add essential oil drops to help relieve nasal congestion. Cotton or flannel are the most delicate and softest on the nose. 

4. Straws. Plastic straws may seem harmless, but we use and throw away a lot of them. 500 million a day, to be exact. In Boulder the new rule is that you need to ask for one and you do not get one by default anymore.Everywhere else,  kindly refuse it when they offer it to you. If you like drinking with a straw, get your own reusable one! There are several stainless steel and glass alternatives available at most supermarkets. You can keep them in your purse or backpack and use them when you want. Make sure they come with a cleaning brush, so you don’t have to worry about mystery particles camping out in them. 

5. Avoid single use polystyrene foam products.  Polystyrene (styrofoam) food containers can leach styrene, a known neurotoxin and probable cancer causing chemical into our food and drinks. The foam breaks down into smaller and smaller pieces, but we never really get rid of it. Basically, the 530,000 tons trashed in the US every year are bad for you and the environment. Take your own reusable containers to restaurants for leftovers and avoid taking out from places that use foam containers.

6. Beer containers. Colorado is a beer lover’s paradise. The best way to package beer is in your own reusable growler or growlette! Most breweries in Boulder allow you to buy one, and simply bring it back to refill.  Both glass and aluminum are better than plastic, but keep in mind that the plastic wrapping around some cans is very confusing for the sorting machines we have in Boulder, so remove it before recycling the cans. Additionally, consider that when buying six packs, the plastic holders are not all created equal: You can find rigid ones (which are recyclable!) or floppy ones (which are not!). If you end up with the floppy ones, be sure to cut up all the holes as well, to prevent them from trapping wildlife.

7. Toiletries. The bathroom is usually the second most wasteful part of anyone’s house. We use so many products, and many are not recyclable! Even worse, they are filled with tons of harmful chemicals that our bodies definitely don’t need. Most items cannot be purchased in bulk, but making homemade toiletries is often really simple and will again save us money! Email me if you want some easy recipes.
Last week, Boulder Public Library had a talk about skincare with kitchen products. You can find the recording and a list of ingredients here. If you don’t have time to make your own homemade toiletries, don’t fret. Support companies who choose sustainable packaging like cardboard, glass, or metal. And be careful with the ingredients. Take a look at the Good Guide website and download the app! It can be the best ally to select your toiletries!

RECYCLING ACTIVITIES
  • Have fun while learning with your younger kids. Disco’s here, dat goes there! Take a Brain Break with Eco-Cycle and Jeff and Paige! Eco-Cycle Stars Captain Zero Waste, Green Star, Eco-Elf, Kilowatt Kid, and Wonder Wormie help Jeff and Paige re-cycle and compost in a disco brain break! Watch the video!
  • Do a Recycling Audit with your older kids or teenagers. Look through your refrigerator, cabinets, and bathroom. Check what items you find that can and can’t be recycled. Use it as a starting point to rethink with them your shopping habits and involve them in the choices you make.
  • Make your own recycling guideline posters with your kids. Using art supplies you have at home (eg. scrap paper, construction paper, poster board markers, crayons) make your own recycling guideline posters to put on your refrigerator or above your recycle bin. Get creative and tape on objects, like a soda can to your poster to show the actual item to be recycled. 
    • Step 1: Find your local recycling guidelines to see what is recyclable in your community. Take a look at the resources I showed in the topic of the week section. 
    • Step 2: Decide where to post your guidelines. Can you hang it in the kitchen or near your recycle bin? 
    • Step 3: Gather your materials, like poster board or cardboard, construction paper, markers, glue, tape, etc. If you’d like to make your poster 3-dimensional, you could tape or glue lightweight materials like aluminum cans, cartons, paper, etc. to your recycling guidelines poster! 
    • Step 4: Create your poster and post it in your home.

GOOD NEWS OF THE WEEK

On Friday, June 12th, Colorado State House voted 42-23 to pass Senate Bill 55: Incentivize the Development of Recycling End Markets. The bill, once signed into law by Governor Polis, will establish a recycling end market development center for the state. This center will attract new businesses and entrepreneurs who use recycled materials to Colorado through technical and financial assistance. The goal is to create more end markets for recycled materials in Colorado, which would reduce the need to transport materials out of state - lowering costs for recyclers and shrinking the carbon footprint of recycling. 

Approximately $265 million worth of paper, glass, metal and plastic is buried each year in Colorado landfills. With the passage of Senate Bill 55, Colorado will move toward a more sustainable future where these materials are instead collected and made into new products right here in the state, creating more jobs and reducing greenhouse gas emissions. If Colorado increases its recycling by just 10%, the emissions reductions would be equivalent to taking 40,000 cars off the road for a year.
