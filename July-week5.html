Dear Residents,

This week the newsletter is going to be in a different and shorter form. I am taking a break and I hope I can give you some suggestions to do the same.

I strongly believe that people won’t protect what they don’t know and love. That’s why pushing people to get closer to nature is integral to any environmental effort. This week, take a moment to discover, nurture or rekindle a sense of awe for the outdoors. Go for a walk, venture on a long hike, enjoy a bike ride. If you have time, experience camping and take a longer trip to one of the protected areas of Colorado or the neighbor states. It does not take a lot - often you just need to change perspective. Here are some suggestions:

  1. Take a walk. Turn off your phone, take a deep breath and walk paying attention to what is around you. Visit usual places focusing on your breath and zooming out to your surroundings, specifically to things that delight and surprise you.
  2. Meditate in the great outdoors. Find a comfy seated or lying position in a safe place outdoors, close your eyes and focus your attention on what you feel and sense. Notice sounds, tastes, smells and sensations.
  3. Experience a natural phenomenon. Wake up early to check the comet this week. Go for a hike at higher elevation to enjoy the high mountain wild flowering season. Plan a visit to the Rocky Mountain National Park in fall, when elks are rutting. Just to give some examples.
  4. Go on a microadventure. Take a map and point to a random village next by and go there. Go for a trail you have never tried. It does not need to be long, just new and different. It can even just be the exploration of a new area of Boulder you have never been to.
  5. Change your perspective. Cultivate wonder and amplify your senses. Wear some pink glasses, literally. Or use a magnifying lens to check out little details you find on your path. If you have kids, try to put yourself at their level and look at the world with their eyes.
  6. Look up. Spend a bit of time checking the sky at night in a dark area. If you go camping, it can be the perfect occasion. This week the comet is giving an additional incentive to do so!
  7. Go explore one of those protected areas that are around. Enjoy being surrounded by nature, seeing wild life, breath in the silence. If history is more your thing, take a look at historic sites and learn something about the past of this area. Grab the energy that this exploration gives you and use it to clear your mind and recharge. In the following you can find some information on places that you can explore in Colorado.

The positive effects of being in nature are well know. And it does not need to be something fancy or extreme. Take a look at this article about the effects of green spaces in cities, and how they are essential for our everyday wellbeing.

Below, I provide some suggestions on places where to go in Colorado and some tips for a sustainable outdoor time. I hope they might be helpful, if not for this week, for the future.

Enjoy the outdoors! I am looking forward to next week’s newsletter, which will go back to our discovery of the food system!

 
NATIONAL PARK SERVICE

We live in an amazing state, and an amazing country. Natural beauties are the monuments of this continent and the effort to protect them has been going on for long: On August 25, 1916, President Woodrow Wilson signed the "Organic Act" creating the National Park Service. The National Park System has since expanded to 419 units (often referred to as parks), more than 150 related areas, and numerous programs that assist in conserving the nation's natural and cultural heritage for the benefit of current and future generations.

Colorado is home of 16 federal protected areas. Of those, we have:

  • 13 National Parks Service protected areas (4 of them are National Parks),
  • 3 National Heritage Areas,
  • 1 Wild & Scenic Rivers Managed by NPS
  • 4 National Trails Administered by NPS
  • 1,583 National Register of Historic Places Listings
  • 25 National Historic Landmarks
  • 15 National Natural Landmarks
  • 1 World Heritage Sites

Cool, right? You can find all information about them here.

Additionally, we have State Parks, State Forests, and other local preserved areas. You can take a look at how much land of Colorado is protected in this map. It looks quite impressive! If you want suggestions for you next adventure in one of the 41 State Parks, take a look at this webpage. Boulder County gives additional information on local protected areas that are part of the Park and Recreation department here. Protected areas are cornerstones of local, national and international conservation strategies. By way of these designations, lands and waters are set aside in-perpetuity to preserve functioning natural ecosystems, act as refuges for species, and maintain ecological processes. Complimentary conservation strategies preserve land for the sustainable use of natural resources, or for the protection of significant geologic and cultural features or open space.

 
TIPS FOR A SUSTAINABLE OUTDOOR TIME

When you are enjoying nature, keep in mind that the respect for your surrounding, wild animals and plants is part of the experience. The idea is to feel part of nature and change the usual perspective that sees us separated from it. The rule that should guide you in this is Leave no trace. What does that mean? That when you leave the place there should be no sign of your presence there. Leave nothing but footsteps, take nothing but pictures! How do you do that?

  • Plan ahead and prepare.
  • Travel and camp in designated areas and on durable surfaces.
  • Dispose of waste properly.
  • Leave what you find.
  • Respect wildlife.
  • Be considerate of other visitors.
  • If you make a fire, check local fire restrictions and minimize the campfire impacts (and be careful with fire, especially in Colorado!).

 

Most parks have rules and regulations, check them out before enjoying the area and be sure to follow them!

Did you know that it is actually possible to be almost zero waste in your camping trips? It is easy to think that camping or picnicking outdoor is the only moment when using disposable items is normal and practical. But it is so much cheaper and so much more sustainable to be zero waste! Here are some easy tips:

  1. Avoid disposables. Use what you have at home. Who says that metal cutlery are not done for camping?
  2. Think about your choice of beverages. Most parks and campgrounds give the possibility to refill your water bottle and in most cases that water is so good! You don’t need to get plastic bottles for your trip. For long hikes in places where you can actually find water, filters are the best option: You can avoid plastic and even avoid to carry big quantities of liquids with you!
  3. Plan ahead for food. You don’t need to eat just snacks and precooked food for a camping trip. Plan for easy meals, with just 4-5 ingredients. You can even prepare some ingredients at home, for example premix pancake dry ingredients in a jar and just add eggs during your trip, or prepare a spice mix in a jar that you can bring from home and use to season that amazing BBQ. Be very aware of how much food you can eat. Leftovers in a camping trip are not very easy to properly conserve.
  4. Plan ahead for toiletries. If you go to a campground with showers, you don’t need special version of what you use at home, you can simply use your usual toiletries. If you want to save space, think about reusing some small jars or containers that you can fill with the soap and shampoo that you have in your bathroom. If you plan a backcountry trip, check for compatible alternatives. Natural bio-gradable solutions can be also package free and be actually very convenient weight wise.
  5. How about the equipment? Anything and everything you need, you can find secondhand. We have these amazing secondhand outdoor shops in Boulder, Sports Recycler and PlayItAgain. You could even just borrow someone else’s gear. Why buy? Unless camping is something you plan to do several times a year, borrowing would be the best option. Some retailers like REI even do equipment rentals. If you do plan to buy, buy quality. Don’t waste your money on cheap versions that will break easily. At the end this saves money in the long term.
  6. Dispose of your trash properly. Do not leave anything in nature. In most parks you can recycle cans, bottles and paper. Do not burn or bury your trash
