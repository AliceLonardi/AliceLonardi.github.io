TOPIC OF THE WEEK: SUSTAINABLE FOOD SHOPPING

In the last few weeks we discovered how agriculture can either be thea cause of global warming and degradation of our ecosystem. But, it can also be part of the environmental solution by creating  sustainable practices and even a way to fight global warming. How can we, as consumers, make choices that support sustainable practices and producers, and buy food that is better for people, animals and the environment? How can we know how our food is produced and its impact on the planet? This week’s newsletter is a mini guide to help you shop for food while keeping in mind sustainability. I have already given some suggestions, in the previous newsletters, on how to make more sustainable choices in meat consumption and how to support more sustainable crop production. Today, I am going deeper into some of those suggestions. 

One of the first inputs I gave was “Buy local”. Transportation accounts for just 11% of the food industry’s greenhouse gas emissions. Why is buying local so important then? Local farms are typically small, independent and family-owned and operated. Family farms strive to preserve the surrounding environment and have a vested interest in their local economies. Buying from local farmers ensures that our money goes to those who need it – small farmers committed to sustainable practices. Additionally, we can talk to the farmers and we can directly ask them more about how they produce our food, sometimes we can even visit their farm! This direct contact with the producer makes it so easy to check on the sustainability of the food we purchase. 

The best ways to buy local food isare from farm stands and farmers markets. 

  • Boulder has a biweekly farmers market (on Saturday from 8am to 2pm and on Wednesday from 4pm to 8pm), where you can find all types of produce, dairy products, eggs and meat. The sellers must be the producers, so you can take advantage of this direct contact: Farmers take pride in their products and are generally happy to answer questions about how they raise their animals and grow their fruits and vegetables. 
  • We are also lucky enough to be surrounded by a lot of farms that practice agriculture in wonderfully sustainable ways. Last week I mentioned the Ollin Farms, which is a good friend of Graduate and Family Housing, as we collaborate with them on the CSA program. They use regenerative practices, producing high quality food while increasing the diversity and health of the ecosystem. You can read a lot about their work and their projects on their website (the project 95 is compelling!). Ollin Farms is not the only farm working in this way. You can find a list of the farms around Boulder and their website, where normally you can get information about their way of producing. Use that as a starting point to select your farm!
  • When you trust a farm, consider joining their Community supported agriculture (CSA) program. In most CSAs, members purchase a CSA share from the farmer before the growing season, and in return receive regular installments of food, usually weekly, for the duration of the season. Many CSAs are limited to vegetables and fruit, but others offer eggs, meat, milk, cheese and even prepared foods. This is the best way to support a farm and get delicious sustainable food!

 

What about items you cannot purchase locally? Or what if you just prefer doing your shopping in a normal grocery store? When you are at the supermarket, keep your eye out for signs and labels on the food. Labels and certifications are developed to help consumers make more aware choices, but they can be overwhelming in number and diversity. According to cataloger ecolabelindex.com, approximately 432 labeling schemes are available in 246 countries, of which 148 include standards for food/beverage. Keep in mind that not all the labels are the same: Some certifications indicate that the food meets certain requirements and they are guaranteed by trustful organizations and institutions. Some of the others are generic and not consistent, and their meaning might be sometimes misleading. So how do you navigate this maze? Some organizations have made excellent guides to help consumers understand the validity of labels and the details of what is behind them.

  • You can find the guide from the Foodprint on this website. It is the most comprehensive guide available: They have details about top pick labels for each food category, they give suggestions on additional labels to look for (with additional certifications on the ethic side of the production) and they warn against labels that lack clear standards and are not completely trustworthy. 
  • Greenerchoices gives a lot of details on some specific labels and certifications, including how verified that label is and how meaningful and consistent it is.
  • This article gives details and evaluates the nine most common food labels, explaining their meaning and their validity. The labels considered are Natural, Organic, Grassfed, Non-GMO, Free range, Gluten Free, No added sugars, Cage free, and Whole grain.

Based on those guides, here are some general directions you can follow to make informed choices.

  • If produce, pay attention to the state or country of origin. Is it labeled Organic? Does the store have an Organic section?
  • If meat, look for labels like Animal Welfare Approved, Certified Grassfed or Certified Organic. 
  • If eggs, look for labels like Organic or Animal Welfare Approved.
  • If your supermarket does not provide guidance on produce origins, or if you wish they carried more local and sustainable products throughout the store, speak with the manager. Encourage your friends and neighbors to do the same. Supermarkets respond to customer demand, and you might convince them that it is worth their while to shift their purchasing.

Curious to know more about this topic? Here are some useful resources.

  • This excellent article gives all possible details about what is behind food labels. It also gives some overview on the most common labels. 

  • More details about labels for fish are given on the WWF website and on the foodandwaterwatch website. 

  • The United States Department of Agriculture (USDA) gives details about labels for meat and poultry. More details about meat and dairy labels can also be found on the Environmental Working Group (EWG) website.

  • The ecolabel index lists all 148 labels on food existing around the world.

  • Food labels are in constant evolution. If you want to go deeper in the topic, take a look at this interesting article on the future of environmental sustainability labelling on food products as a starting point.

 
TIPS OF THE WEEK

Want to be more sustainable with your food choices? Here are some suggestions on how to be more effective

  1. Buy local. Again, to be sure about the footprint of your food, it’s the best to get to know your farm, talk with them about their agricultural practices and support them if they have standards that you consider good enough.
  2. Visit farmers markets or consider a CSA program. 
  3. Learn as much as you can about food labels and certifications. Some are meaningful and trustworthy, others less. Take a look at the guides listed in the resources above. Use them to guide your choices at the grocery shop.
