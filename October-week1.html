Dear residents,

last week several people subscribed to the mailing list to get the Sustainability Newsletter, so I decided to give you an additional week to challenge yourself with the carbon footprint quiz I suggested last week! 

You can take a look at last week’s newsletter to have some background on Carbon Dioxide Emissions and Carbon Footprint, what they are and why they matter when discussing the impact of human activities on the planet. You can find it in the Sustainability Newsletter Archive. The quiz proposed is very difficult and is meant to show how obscure these calculations and these comparisons are. Don't be discouraged! Don’t try to do too much math, trust your feelings and try to make your best guess! One of the main purposes is to check how your intuition goes: When you see the answers you will be able to set it right and you will be able to learn so much on the importance of the single choices that you are making in your daily life to reduce those carbon emissions! Nobody is going to judge your results: All people that submit their answers on the google form will have the same possibility to win a nice prize of their choice from the Refill Revolution, a package free shop for personal care and household items in Boulder, independently from their answers! 

A big thanks to all the people that took the quiz already: A bit more patience for the results (sorry for the big cliffhanger!), next week you will find out how good your answers were!

For this week, you can find the usual suggestions for events, some directly related to sustainability, while others are activities you can enjoy with your friends and family and do not require buying things or traveling far. 

If you have any specific topics you would like to see in one of the next weeks, let me know! This is the email address: gfhsustainability@colorado.edu. Inputs and comments are always very welcome!

Dinosaur Ridge History Walking Tour

Saturday October 10 at       5 PM – 7 PM

You will walk the trail at Dinosaur Ridge, a National Natural Landmark, and explore the fascinating geology and paleontology of the area, traveling all the way back to the Jurassic! Along the way, you will learn about the time of the Bone Wars in late 1800s wild west paleontology, touch dozens of dinosaur fossils from 150 million years ago, witness the result of the earth-shattering geologic uplift that formed the Rocky Mountains, and find trace fossils of an ancient inland ocean that covered the area in the Cretaceous.

Wildfire smoke and air quality

Thursday October 8, at 7 PM – 8 PM

How do scientists study wildfires? Why does smoke from wildfires travel such great distances and how does it impact air quality, nutrient cycles, weather and climate? In this Explorer Series Conversation, you can talk with scientists from the field project Western Wildfire Experiment for Cloud Chemistry, Aerosol Absorption and Nitrogen (WE-CAN). Join the conversation to learn more about wildfires, how changes in climate have increased the impact of wildfires, and what it’s like to be a student and scientist collecting data from a flying laboratory.

GOOD NEWS OF THE WEEK

Coronavirus Countermeasures Turn Back Carbon Emissions 10 Years
As a result of countermeasures taken amid the Covid-19 pandemic, researchers are getting a glimpse of a world with less demand for fossil fuels. Widespread lockdowns and restrictions on economic activity have dwindled the level of worldwide carbon dioxide emissions. We are seeing "a world in which renewable energy makes up a greater share of energy use, and carbon emissions are turned back to levels from a decade ago," but the long-term effects of these gratifying numbers depend on the "recovery paths" we will take post-pandemic.
