TOPIC OF THE WEEK: RECYCLE 2.0

After I sent the newsletter last week I got several emails asking me questions on how to handle specific items. And I am aware that the list of materials I gave was not exhaustive and it did not cover all things you can recycle in Boulder! For these reasons, I decided to stay another week on the topic of recycling and I hope this newsletter can be a mini-guide for you to be a real recycling master in Boulder!

I think the best way to start is getting aware of the system behind the recycling process. Why can you recycle a plastic bottle but not plastic wrap, even if they are made of the same material? This is due to the sorting system that Eco-Cycle is using to separate the stuff that you put in the recycling dumpster. In the past years the sustainability liaison organized a tour to take a look at Eco-Cycle. This year, tours are unfortunately suspended due to COVID-19. But you can take a look at this video and do a virtual tour of the Eco-Cycle facility! I think this is a great way to understand the sorting mechanisms and it makes it easier to decide what you can (and can't) recycle.

The above considerations are valid for curbside recycling only, meaning the stuff you can put in the recycling dumpsters directly outside of your apartment. In Boulder we additionally have the possibility to take care of the so called “Hard to recycle” materials. Did you know we have a dedicated center for that? The Center for HArd to Recycle Materials (CHaRM) is located at 6400 Arapahoe Avenue and it accepts many items that you can’t directly put in the recycling bins:

  • Electronics, like computers, laptops, TVs, monitors, mobile phones, audio equipment and Printer Cartridges: Some things are accepted free of charge (for example laptops or computers), for others you need to pay a fee. Keep in mind that you CANNOT put electronics in the landfill dumpster, it is illegal in Colorado as electronics contain toxic substances.
  • Hard-to-Recycle Plastics
    • Plastic Bags and Bubble Wraps, like grocery and retail shopping bags, food storage bags (ziplock bags, produce bags, bread bags), dry cleaning bags, newspaper sleeves and bags, package overwrap (from toilet paper, paper towels, napkins, diapers, etc.), case stretch wrap (from bulk snacks, water bottles, etc.), cereal box liners, shipping envelopes, bubble wraps, air pillows.
    • Foam Packing Sheets
    • White Block Foam Packaging (no food styrofoam!)
    • Big Durable Plastics, like toys, plastic furniture, watering cans, laundry baskets, buckets
  • Appliances
    • Small Plastic Appliances: Electric and battery-powered appliances, with a fee
    • Metal Appliances: Items at least 50% metal. Examples are microwaves or water boilers. Appliances that contain Freon (like air conditioners or dehumidifiers) are also accepted with a fee.
  • Scrap Metal, like pots or pans.
  • Mattresses and Box Springs: There is a fee for these.
  • Bicycles and Parts, Bike Tires and Tubes: There is a small fee for the tires and tubes.
  • Cooking Oil: Up to 10 gallons of any type is accepted.
  • Yoga Mats
  • Textiles, like clothes, towels, sheets, fabrics, shoes. They need to be clean. Due to COVID-19 textiles are not accepted at CHaRM at this moment, but they will resume the service during the summer.

You can find all specific information and prices here. For some of these items there are alternative ways for recycling outside CHaRM. Some electronics and appliances are also accepted in electronics stores, like BestBuy or Staples. Take a look at their specific recycling programs, you can save money for the next purchase! CU has a special program for Cell Phone Recycling, with several dropoff boxes on campus. Most grocery shops accept plastic bags and plastic wraps. Shops, like Community Cycles, accept old bikes and parts. Specific clothing brands have recycling or upcycling programs and they accept items back. Try to find out about those BEFORE buying clothes, and use those programs to do more sustainable shopping! And for most of these items, if functioning and in good conditions, donating to second hand and thrift stores in Boulder is the best possibility!

In Boulder we also have a recycling center for Hazardous material, Boulder County Hazardous Materials Management Facility (HMMF). There you can bring all products found in your kitchen, bathroom, garage, or garden shed that are potentially hazardous substances. Examples include motor oil, paints, pesticides, antifreeze, wood preservatives, many household cleaners that contain solvents, petroleum products, heavy metals, or other toxic chemicals. You can find a complete list and additional information on HMMF (and extra tips on hazardous products!). It is extra important to bring these chemicals to the HMMF: because of their toxicity they can be very harmful for the environment and for people! In Graduate and Family Housing you can easily recycle your batteries and light bulbs: There are battery recycling bins in all laundry rooms and you can call Maintenance for light bulbs.

Additional recycling possibilities? Something that is getting more and more common are companies that accept their own packaging or products back after use. Ask me for examples if you want more information! Another incredibly cool initiative is TerraCycle. You can send them items that are normally very problematic for recycling and they take care of them, such as snack wrappers or coffee cups. In Boulder there are at least two locations where they accept items and send them to TerraCycle: REI and Flatiron Subaru.

CU has a cool program to recycle and reuse office supplies. Reusable supplies are made available for students at the beginning of each semester. The materials accepted are notebooks and journals, folders and dividers, 3-ring binders, pans, pencils, markers, sticky notes and notepads, office organizers, new printer cartridges and ink jets, tyvek envelopes, transparencies, and interdepartmental mail envelopes. You can send these things to 209 UCB, Boulder, CO, 80309 or bring them to the Environmental Center in UMC 355.

TIPS OF THE WEEK

The big suggestion this week is to take a Plastic-Free July Challenge! For 10 years, people around the world have come together for a month of awareness on plastic pollution, sharing solutions and suggestions to reduce our impact on the environment and acting on limiting the use of single-use plastic items. You can take a look at the web page, subscribe for the challenge and get information on how to get involved. The Eco-Cycle is also offering Plastic-Free July Challenge support: you can subscribe here. Remember, you don’t need to subscribe to anything to take the challenge! All you have to do is commit to one month of trying to avoid disposable plastic. You can challenge yourself and commit at different levels. Use the suggestions I gave in the last weeks and this week to help with the challenge.

This is the third week of tips to switch from disposable items to reusable or plastic free alternatives. Again, try to focus on one of those options this week and maybe go to the next ones in the following weeks. If you want to do it all in the next 7 days then, fantastic, the planet loves overachievers!

1. Picnic. This weekend is going to be the 4th of July. What better to celebrate than an outdoor picnic with friends? And what better occasion to inspire people to go Zero Waste? Organize your picnic with friends telling them you are trying to go Zero Waste. Be sure to bring along food and drink that’s not wrapped in plastic. You can do this easily by buying food from local bulk food stores, checking out the farmers market, and making it yourself from scratch.

  • Bake your own crackers – there are dozens of great recipes online.
  • Bring water in a reusable bottle (and encourage others to do the same!)
  • If choosing to bring soft drinks, consider cans instead of plastic bottles or DIY with a fizzy drink maker.
  • Make sandwiches and put them in reusable containers or wax wraps.
  • Bring along nuts and treats from the local bulk food store.
  • Buy corn on the cob and make delicious popcorn at home. Bring it along in a reusable container.
  • Stock up on cheese at the farmers market or any local cheese shop – remember to BYO container.
  • Bake a cake and bring it along as a sweet treat.
  • Bring reusable cutlery, plates and glasses. You can encourage everyone to bring their own (which saves you on washing up). Remember to bring some spares just in case anyone forgets.

2.Shop Meat, Fish and Deli. Those products are often sold on polystyrene trays; a type of plastic that is extremely difficult to recycle. It also contains chemicals that can be harmful to human health. It’s worth shopping around for local butchers, fishmongers, or delis that sell unpackaged items. Farmers markets or farm stands are perfect for that! Just remember to bring your own reusable container along when you go shopping.ed!

3. Food Storage and Plastic Wraps. Plastic cling wrap is a very common item in most homes. Many products are pre-packed using it, too. Alternatives exist that are just as effective, and more sustainable. Storing and packing food in reusable containers is a great start. You can also purchase reusable silicone bowl toppers to cover food in bowls, pots and pans, reusable cloth cover or wax wrap, or even use a simple upside down plate. Wax wraps and containers make a great, reusable alternative for wrapping sandwiches as well. You can also use your reusable washable cloth bags to store and pack food, as a better option to ziplock bags.

4. Snacks. If you have strong snacking habits, thinking package-free alternatives might not be super easy and it heavily depends on your taste. Consider that most alternatives can have the big advantage of being healthier, so if you don’t want to do it for the environment think about yourself! Some suggestions for easy snacks that are already available in bulk sections of supermarkets are trail mix or popcorn, or even fruit. Other possibilities I can think of are bread and olive oil, or any kind of bean dips, like hummus. If you have time and you like cooking or baking, the possibilities become endless. Take a look at the recipes suggested by Eco-Cycle in this mini Zero Waste snack guide. Contact me if you want more recipes!

5. Toilet Paper. When using toilet paper we are literally cutting down trees for a single 5 second usage. One tree produces about 200 rolls (100 pounds (45 kg)) of toilet paper and about 83 million rolls are produced per day. Global toilet paper production consumes 27,000 trees daily! It seems almost impossible, but there are more sustainable options for toilet paper as well! If installing a bidet in Graduate and Family Housing is not the easiest possibility and toilet paper cloths are not your thing, consider toilet paper made with recycled content. You can find it in most supermarkets and it is not more expensive than the white toilet paper.

6.Tea. It’s surprising how many tea bag brands (unfortunately) use plastic. Going for loose leaf tea is the obvious alternative when it comes to avoiding the plastic that is in many tea bags. You can bring your own container to a local food store, or make sure you buy loose leaf tea without the extra plastic-packaging. If you want to stick to the old faithful tea bags, though, it’ll just take a bit of research. Doing a search for plastic free tea bags on the Internet is your best bet, as companies are continually changing their policies.

7. Shaving. Disposable razors and shaving cream packaged in plastic contribute to our growing landfill. Finding alternatives is easier than you might think. More and more people are choosing to switch to safety razors, which are generally made of stainless steel and have replaceable blades. While they may be more expensive than disposable razors from the outset, in the long-run they can end up saving you money. Many companies now offer blade refills in card-board boxes, making it a great way to avoid single-use plastic entirely. There are also companies who create shaving bars, sold in cardboard boxes, allowing people to create shaving foam without any plastic packaging.

Moon Eclipse and More

The Fourth of July will feature more than just fireworks. Weather permitting, a lunar eclipse will be visible across North America and South America. In Colorado, viewing conditions will range between good and fair. The eclipse will begin on July 4, at 9:07 p.m. and last until 11:52 p.m. The best time to look will be around 10:30 p.m. during the middle of the event.

The month of July will bring more exciting astronomical events. Around the middle of the month, both Jupiter and Saturn will reach opposition, or the point in their orbits when they are closest to the Earth. The end of the month will feature a pair of meteor showers with the Alpha Capricornids and the southern Delta Aquarids both peaking on the same night (July 28th-29th).

GOOD NEWS OF THE WEEK

Arvada Becomes Largest City in the State to Benefit from a Contracted Organized Trash and Recycling System 

The COVID-19 pandemic has paused several local efforts to improve recycling -- but not in the City of Arvada, which has been actively working on a proposed organized waste hauling ordinance for the last 18 months. 

The ordinance would change the city’s waste collection system from an open market (where 9 different haulers serve residents) to an organized trash and recycling system with a one truck/one street approach. The new service provides a large 96-gal recycling toter for collection every other week at no additional charge and offers volume-based pricing for weekly trash collection. It affects all single-family homes that are not part of HOAs -- about 32,000 households. The City points to safer and quieter streets, less air pollution, less wear and tear on city roads, and affordable hauling rates as just some of the benefits of this program. 

Since the new program provides all participating households with a recycling bin and curbside service at no additional cost, it is expected that Arvada’s recycling rate will more than double from just 13% to 30% or more. Additionally, residents will be charged for trash by the size of cart they use, financially rewarding residents who produce less trash. At a time when many things seem to be “on pause,” Arvada is an example of communities moving forward on a path to Zero Waste! And this win is significant in that Arvada sets an example for other large cities within the state to provide recycling; the more cities that do that, the better our state’s overall recycling and resource recovery rate.
