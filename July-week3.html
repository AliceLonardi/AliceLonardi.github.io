TOPIC OF THE WEEK: FOOD WASTE

Last week we talked about the effects of our organic waste on the environment and why the impact of composting can be so important: If half of what goes into landfills is organic material, taking it out by composting it can really make a difference! But what is this organic waste? 

Quite a lot of it is actually food waste. In the U.S., up to 40% of food goes uneaten. Globally, it is not much better: A third of the food raised or prepared does not make it from farm or factory to fork. Now let’s try to put that in perspective, comparing it to this two facts:

  1. Worldwide, 800 million people face hunger. According to Feeding America, one in 11 of our neighbors in Boulder County struggles with hunger - including more than 6,000 children;
  2. Food waste produces 4.4 gigatons of carbon dioxide equivalent, roughly 8% of the total human greenhouse emissions. If it was a country, food waste would be the third-largest emitter of greenhouse gases globally, right after the U.S. and China! And this does not even consider the impact of food production...

It becomes clear that people who need food are not getting it and food that is getting wasted, all while our planet is heating up! 

The food system is elaborate and complex, and waste happens at every stage. We will spend the next few weeks talking about different aspects, but from these numbers it is already quite clear that something needs to change. Composting, for example, is an amazing way to transform food waste from a greenhouse gas generator to a resource that helps increase soil health and reduce carbon in the atmosphere. But composting does nothing to save  the resources involved in the production of food, like water, soil, and energy. Again, the comparison with trash works very well: The first step to solve the problem is reducing the amount of food we waste before we compost it.

But can WE really do something about it? Yes, we can! In the U.S., households are responsible for the largest portion of all food waste: The estimate is that households waste a total of 76 billion pounds (34.5 billion kg) of food per year. This means that approximately 40 to 50 percent of food waste happens at the level of the consumer, which means us! Can you imagine buying 5 bags full of food and throwing away one of them before even getting home?! On a personal level the numbers are even more impressive: An average person wastes 238 pounds (108 kg) of food per year, which is 21 percent of the food we buy! The impact is also massive at an economical level: Food waste costs every one of us $1,800 per year! It seems that we can actually make quite an impact reducing our food waste!

So what can you do to reduce your food waste?

  1. Learn what you can do at home to help reduce food waste with these cooking with less waste ideas or using these recipes to cook with food scraps or to use food past its prime. 
  2. Try planning meals and shopping in advance: You can use some of those amazing tools, like the Guest-imator, to have an estimate for dinner parties, or the Meal Prep Mate, to create a shopping list and plan meals without wasting.
  3. Learn how to create a zero-waste kitchen. 
  4. If you have a garden plot at GFH and you end up having too much produce, consider donating it! The city of Boulder is pushing the “Grow and Give” project, a sort of modern version of the victory gardens, to reduce food insecurity in the community. You can download the Fresh Food Connect app (you can learn more about it as well) and easily drop your donation off at your convenience! 
  5. Take a look at the tips of the week for more detailed suggestions as well.

  • The Drawdown Project lists the reduction of food waste as the third most impactful solution to reduce climate change. On the dedicated page, you can find technical data about the impact and the different solutions suggested for different parts of the world.

TIPS OF THE WEEK

Do you want ideas to reduce your food waste? Here are some practical suggestions to make it easy! If you have specific questions on this topic, write me an email! 

  1. As already mentioned in the topic of the week part, try meal planning and shopping with a list. You can start using the Meal Prep Mate, one of the resources that you can find on savethefood.com. Think about what meals you will have this week, keep in mind what you already have at home and the leftovers that are still in your fridge, and consider how many people you will serve. Make a list and stick to it when you go grocery shopping. Also, avoid grocery shopping when you are hungry!
  2. Get creative and use more of your produce, learn to use over-ripe fruits or the vegetable peels and discover how to revive produce that looks limp. Leaves of root vegetables make for a very tasty salad and a brown banana can become an amazing smoothie or a refreshing ice-cream! Putting limp celery in water revives it in just a few minutes! You can find suggestions everywhere, here are some examples: "12 ways to use food waste and save money", cooking with less waste ideas or using these recipes to cook with food scraps or to use food past its prime. 
  3. Remember the leftovers! Don’t hide them in the back of the fridge, and consider putting a colorful sticky note with the date on them! Some leftovers can be transformed in a different dish, so you don’t get bored with the same type of food for too many days in a row: Pasta leftovers can be transformed in a frittata just adding some eggs, cooked vegetables make for amazing sauces and cooked rice can become a delicious dessert!
  4. Use proper storage for your food, so you can avoid food going bad too quickly. The savethefood website has an entire page for that, with specific instructions for specific items. Did you know that you can even freeze eggs?!
  5. Understand the date labels and learn how to check when food is not good anymore by yourself, using your eyes, nose and taste to test the quality of your food. Expiration dates are more of a guesstimate and most food is still good for several days, if not weeks after the “best-by” date! This is especially true for dry food, like pasta, rice or flour. 
  6. Compost what you can’t save! Check out the newsletter of last week for all details about composting and write to me if you want to have it but didn’t get it!

GOOD NEWS OF THE WEEK

Vermont set a precedent in waste conservation this month when it became the first U.S. state to ban food scraps in trash. The legislation, which took effect July 1, prohibits residents, restaurants, supermarkets and other businesses from disposing of food scraps - such as peels, rinds, seeds eggshells, coffee grinds and meal leftovers - into trash cans or other containers used for refuse. Read more about it in this article.

But how does it work around the world? Food waste is a global problem, affecting our economy, our well-being and our environment. Approximately one third of all food produced gets wasted, costing the world $1 trillion each year. The good news is that there have been positive changes in recent years and that many governments are taking action to tackle the issue. Governments can enforce change and make all sectors operate to a minimum standard. This makes the war against food waste much easier to win. Here you can find a detailed list of how governments around the world are encouraging food waste initiatives.
