CARBON DIOXIDE EMISSIONS AND CARBON FOOTPRINT

Climate change is one of the biggest issues that the environment and humanity are facing. When we talk about climate change, or sometimes “global warming” (even if technically they are not the same concept), we refer to the fact that the climate on our planet is changing compared to the past, and specifically it is getting globally warmer on average due to human activity. How can humans alter the climate of the Earth? Climate on our planet is the result of a delicate and complex equilibrium of multiple different factors, tightly interconnected: Solar radiation, Earth orbit, atmosphere composition,and ocean influence are just some examples of these factors. If changes occur rapidly or on a big scale in one of those factors, it can change this equilibrium and have a big effect on all the others. This can often start a chain reaction that reinforces the change itself. Since the mid-20th century human activity has changed this equilibrium, having an unprecedented impact on Earth’s climate system. The largest factor impacting the climate has been the emission of greenhouse gases. Greenhouse gases are gases that trap the heat from solar radiation and prevent it from dispersing in space, very similarly to the way a greenhouse traps heat so that plants stay alive year-round. Those gases are naturally present in the atmosphere, and they are actually responsible for the living conditions we have on our planet: without them the Earth's average temperature would be well below the freezing point of water. What is the problem then? The problem is that the concentration of greenhouse gases in the atmosphere increased dramatically from before industrial times. We thickened the greenhouse layer. And as a consequence, too much heat is stored in the atmosphere, destabilizing the delicate equilibrium responsible for the climate. In which way is the climate changing? As said earlier, the average global temperature is increasing. Also the temperature of the oceans is increasing. This increase of temperature caused much of the ice on the Earth surface to melt, including the disappearing of glaciers, shrinking of the ice sheet in Antarctica, Greenland and Arctic Sea, and even the amount of snow we get during the winter is lower than in the past and it is melting earlier during the year. The ice melting causes an increase of the sea level. All of this destabilized the climate at the point that we have more extreme weather events, such as more intense rainfalls, worse droughts, more devastating hurricanes and wildfires, and so on. The consequences for our life on Earth are getting stronger and stronger: it goes from having a more difficult time producing food to having to face those extreme events (think about people having to evacuate because of the wildfires or losing their houses because of hurricanes or floods).The last newsletter I sent out was about pollinators, their amazing work helping produce our food, and how the heavy use of some fertilizers, pesticides, and herbicides are threatening them. Some of the same chemicals in those products are also in the cosmetics we use everyday in our personal care routine. You may think you don’t use cosmetics? Think again. Legally, the term cosmetics includes any products you apply to your body that are not drugs. Think about going to the bathroom in the morning: Do you brush your teeth using toothpaste? How about shampoo and shower-gel for your shower? And how about the soap you use every time you wash your hands during the day, especially now during this pandemic? Toothpaste, shampoo & conditioner, shower-gel, and hand soap are all cosmetics, just like deodorant, sunscreen, hand lotion, lipstick, makeup, and nail polish. A recent survey found that the average adult uses nine cosmetic products every day (12 for women, 6 for men), with a total of 126 different toxic ingredients. We do not eat cosmetics, so that is not a big problem, right?

To be able to do something to fight climate change we need to be very aware of the sources and of the nature of greenhouse gas emissions. Where are those greenhouse gases coming from? Fossil fuel burning for energy consumption is the main source of these emissions, with additional contributions from agriculture, deforestation, and industrial processes. This means that any activity that requires energy is potentially a source of greenhouse gases: Production and consumption of food, manufactured goods, materials, wood, roads, buildings, transportation and other services. But different activities emit different amounts and different types of greenhouse gases. 

Greenhouse gases are carbon dioxide (CO2), methane, tropospheric ozone, CFCs, nitrous oxide and water vapor. Each gas's effect on climate change depends on three main factors: how much of that gas there is in the atmosphere (concentration, or abundance), how long the gas stays in the atmosphere, and how strongly it impacts the atmosphere. Concentration wise, carbon dioxide (CO2) and methane make up to 90% of the greenhouse gases: CO2 is the primary greenhouse gas emitted through human activities – about 80% of the emissions in the US. That is why we have a way to compare all other gases to CO2: For each greenhouse gas, a Global Warming Potential (GWP) has been calculated to reflect how long it remains in the atmosphere, on average, and how strongly it absorbs energy. CO2 has GWP of exactly 1 (since it is the baseline unit to which all other greenhouse gases are compared), most other gases have GWP bigger than 1. For example, methane is a stronger greenhouse gas (about 85 times stronger than CO2), but it stays in the atmosphere for a shorter time: its GWP is 34 over 100 years. GWP gives us a way to calculate the impact of different activities on climate change: Carbon dioxide equivalent, calculated from GWP, is the amount of CO2 which would warm the earth as much as that amount of that gas. From the carbon dioxide equivalent we can calculate the Carbon Footprint, which is nothing more than the total greenhouse gas emissions caused by an activity, but also by an individual, event, organization, service, or product, expressed as carbon dioxide equivalent. When we say for example that a beef burger has a carbon footprint of 700kg (1500 pounds), we consider in this calculation the greenhouse gas emissions from the rearing of cows and the cultivation of cereals (feed for the cows), the production of agricultural inputs, processing in the food industry (butchering and packaging of the meat), as well as transports. Sometimes also the food waste is considered. The methane emissions are included in those 700kg CO2 using the GWP equivalence. The carbon footprint is a super useful tool to evaluate the impact of more or less anything on the environment, and identify the sectors where we need to focus our efforts to reduce emissions. On the other hand, we should keep in mind that the carbon footprint describes only the greenhouse gas emissions, and does not evaluate any other environmental impacts, like effects on biodiversity, or social and cultural impacts, on health, communities and cultures. As any other tools there are limits and for sure it cannot become the only parameter to guide our environmental effort.
