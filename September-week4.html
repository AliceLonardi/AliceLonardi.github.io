TOPIC OF THE WEEK: COSMETICS AND PERSONAL CARE PRODUCTS

The last newsletter I sent out was about pollinators, their amazing work helping produce our food, and how the heavy use of some fertilizers, pesticides, and herbicides are threatening them. Some of the same chemicals in those products are also in the cosmetics we use everyday in our personal care routine. You may think you don’t use cosmetics? Think again. Legally, the term cosmetics includes any products you apply to your body that are not drugs. Think about going to the bathroom in the morning: Do you brush your teeth using toothpaste? How about shampoo and shower-gel for your shower? And how about the soap you use every time you wash your hands during the day, especially now during this pandemic? Toothpaste, shampoo & conditioner, shower-gel, and hand soap are all cosmetics, just like deodorant, sunscreen, hand lotion, lipstick, makeup, and nail polish. A recent survey found that the average adult uses nine cosmetic products every day (12 for women, 6 for men), with a total of 126 different toxic ingredients. We do not eat cosmetics, so that is not a big problem, right?

Again, reconsider. Using cosmetics on a daily basis means that our body absorbs the ingredients that are in those cosmetics, mainly through the skin. Our skin is our interface with the external environment and is the first line of defense from external factors. Did you know that the skin is also our body's biggest organ!? But our skin is not completely impermeable: It absorbs about 60% of what it gets in contact with, this value varies a lot depending on the substance. It depends  on which part of the skin, on the amount of time the substance is in contact with the skin itself, and so on. Children absorb as much as 40% to 50% more than adults. In fact, it is calculated that with the use of cosmetics, our body absorbs nearly five pounds (2.3 kg) of chemicals a year! The range of those chemicals include carcinogens, pesticides, endocrine disruptors, neurotoxins and reproductive toxins, that often remain on the market despite known toxic effects. Most of us use these products without a thought as to their safety. In the USA, according to FDA regulations, cosmetic companies are responsible for the health and safety of their own products. However, only 20% of the ingredients normally used in cosmetics have been assessed for safety by the industry safety panel. The FDA is allowed to take products deemed unsafe off the market, but as of today this had happened for 11 out of over 12,000 cosmetics ingredients since 1938! At the same time, EU has banned well over a thousand different personal product ingredients that have been linked to cancer, birth defects, genetic mutation and/or reproductive harm. In recent years concerns have been raised about several ingredients used in cosmetics, some of which are turning up in our bodies in unexpected amounts. What does this mean for your health, and how can you choose safer products?

While this information may feel overwhelming, never fear! First of all, check the ingredients of any cosmetics you may want to buy. Although many companies are now jumping on the Green Movement bandwagon and marketing specific “all-natural” and “eco-friendly” products, check the ingredients to make sure their claims are true. There’s no government regulation on these types of phrases, unlike organics, so no one is checking up on these claims. This is also true for things like laundry detergent and cleaning supplies, so be aware of what you buy and what you are exposing yourself to when you clean your clothes or your apartment! Take some time to think about all the products you use during your average day and see how many of the things you use are really safe and eco-friendly. This could expand to things you had never even thought of, like tampons and pads. Below are some commonly used toxins, some of their uses, and some of their effects (Information from “Cosmetic Poisoning Statistics from Skin Care Products”). Keep those in mind when you buy cosmetics next time!

  • Benzoyl peroxide (common in acne products): May promote tumor growth, mutagenesis (mutated cells), is known to cause DNA damage in some cells, can be toxic if inhaled, irritates eyes, skin, and respiratory tract, and may create chemical sensitivity over time.
  • DEA, MEA, and TEA (di/mono/tri ethanolamine, common foaming agent in skin care products): Can cause contact dermatitis, which is when the skin becomes red, swollen, and sore, similar to an allergic reaction; Causes organ system toxicity, meaning that DEA accumulates in the liver and kidney, causing organ toxicity and also possible neurotoxic effects such as tremors
  • FD&C Colors and Pigments (synthetic colors): Many toxins left in the skin, can cause skin irritation, its absorption can deplete oxygen in the blood, is shown to cause cancer in many studies;
  • Sunscreen Chemicals (common in the U.S. - Includes avobenzone, benzophenone, ethoxycinnamate, PABA): Cause free radical generation, which are created by oxidation, in excess they will accelerate your aging and lead to negative health problems, may damage DNA or lead to cancer
  • Parabens (common in skin products)(methyl/butyl/ethyl/propyl parabens):  may contribute to early puberty, female hormone problems, male sterility.

Several apps have been developed to help consumers understand the products they purchase. They are very practical for shopping, as you can avoid reading the entire ingredient list and just scan a product getting a rate for their safety and environmental impact. I am listing some of them in the Suggestions of the Week! This might be the easiest way to handle your cosmetic selection!

 

Additional to the health problems, a big concern with these types of products is the environmental impact, at different levels.

1. A big part of personal and house care products ends up in our water system: When you take a shower and you rinse off that shampoo from your body, it goes down the drain. The same for the laundry detergent from the washing machine or the soap that you wash out your dishes. How long do they stay in the water system and how do they affect it? 

2. What about the production impact of those products? How are the basic ingredients produced, extracted or grown? Some of the chemicals are based on petrochemicals, and they have the same impact as plastic. Some others are literally plastics: Think for example of the microbeads found in some exfoliants. The extraction of some chemicals have devastating effects on the environment and on the communities living near the extraction sites. The production of some natural ingredients can be monoculture type and have a strong effect on the soil.

3. And how about the packaging of those products? Cosmetics are often packaged in containers composed of mixed materials (e.g. a pump-action bottle made with different plastic resins and a metal spring). These packages are often impossible to recycle due to high separation, cleaning, and processing costs. For example, menstruation is a part of every female’s life but all of the plastic and cotton (or rayon) wasted by using three tampons or pads a day for a week really adds up.

TIPS OF THE WEEK

Do you want to make sure that your personal and house care products are really safe and eco-friendly? Take a look at those suggestions!

  1. Take some time to think about all the products you use during your average day and check the ingredients, origin, and sustainability. Note that even if you trusted a producer about the use of non-toxic ingredients in your home county, it does not mean that the same products here are ok. Multinational companies adapt the ingredients to the regulations they find, and toxic ingredients might be cheaper. 
  2. Learn to recognize the most toxic chemicals and make a habit out of reading the ingredient list of products before buying them. This website works as an ingredient dictionary: It is extremely useful to check specific ingredients, giving a rating, explaining the uses and providing references. 
  3. Take a look at alternative stores that sell cosmetics and cleaning products in bulk: You can avoid a big amount of packaging by reusing your bottles and containers! One of those shops is Refill Revolution, located on Arapahoe Avenue in Boulder! Other suggestions to make your personal care habits more sustainable concerning waste were given in several past newsletters and you can find all of them in the Archive!
  4. These are the most common apps developed to guide consumers for safer and more sustainable shopping. Download one or more of them and use them to guide your choices: 
    • Think Dirty can scan barcodes, or search by type or brand to help you find out more information about your items before you buy them. It will generate its "Dirty Meter" report on the ingredients and offer suggestions for cleaner alternatives. The database contains over 550,000 products and you can feel good about shopping clean. The app is free and easy to use.
    • The Skin Deep website and Healthy Living App are part of the Environmental Working Group. That org's mission is to "empower people to live healthier lives in a healthier environment. With breakthrough research and education, we drive consumer choice and civic action." The group uses the "EWG VERIFIED" mark. When a product has this distinction, you know it's free from EWG's chemical concerns.
    • Detox Me is also free and uses research to help reduce a consumer's exposure to potentially harmful chemicals in all household products, including beauty items. The apps' ethos is that knowledge is "a prescription for prevention." It allows you to track your progress in terms of eliminating toxic products, offers tips and recommendations for safer alternatives, and breaks down those often confusing labels and technical terminology.
  5. Consider making some of those products yourself! For some products, this is really easy: a super effective multi-surface cleaning product can be made with only vinegar, lemon and baking soda! For others the process takes a bit longer, but the results are cosmetics that you can potentially even eat and are super safe! You can find some tutorial from the Boulder Public Library: in this you can learn to make sunscreen or this one is about herbal skincare from your kitchen. Several others are available online or you can write to me for specific suggestions!

GOOD NEWS OF THE WEEK

Key House Committee Approves Landmark Cosmetics Safety Legislation
Members of the House Energy and Commerce Committee’s Subcommittee on Health approved landmark cosmetics safety legislation, a significant step toward finally protecting consumers from potentially toxic chemical ingredients in personal care products. The bill would, among other provisions, require cosmetics companies to substantiate the safety of their products, notify the Food and Drug Administration of any adverse health events, give the FDA the power to conduct its own safety reviews, and mandate that manufacturers provide more transparency about ingredients on their labels.
