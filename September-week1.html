TOPIC OF THE WEEK: SUSTAINABLE FOOD SHOPPING 2.0

Last week I started a mini guide to help you shop for food while keeping sustainability in mind: We discovered why it is important to buy local food and the options we have in Boulder, and how to recognize sustainable food in grocery shops taking advantage of labels and certifications. This week you can find more suggestions on how to select your food based on seasonality, why cooking at home makes for an easier way to eat with a lower impact on the environment and how to make choices that keep this impact in mind when dining out.

An important point of eating in a sustainable way, and strongly connected with buying local, is eating in season. Seasonal food is produce that is purchased and consumed around the time that it is harvested. Think about the delicious peaches that you find in Colorado in summer or getting pumpkins for Halloween! The concept of seasonality is normally referred to produce, but also fish, meat and cheese can have seasonal cycles. Why is buying seasonal food so important? The first advantage of choosing seasonal food is that it is easier to be sure that it is locally produced. Producing peaches in December is virtually impossible for a farm in Colorado. When you get them in the supermarket in winter, those peaches need to travel a long way to get to your table. And have you ever noticed the difference in taste? This is the second point: Seasonal food is fresher, tastier and more nutritious than food consumed out of season. Out of season produce is harvested early because it needs to be shipped and distributed to the local stores. It gets ripe during the transport or even in your kitchen after you purchase it. Seasonal and local crops are picked at their peak of ripeness, because they don’t need to survive long travels and they are consumed shortly after the harvest (sometimes just a few hours after!). This makes them better tasting and full of flavor and nutrients, as they are allowed to ripen naturally on their parent plant. Additionally, seasonal food is more likely produced following natural cycles. For produce, this means that no resource intensive practices to extend the growing time was used. For fish this helps keep the seas and fishing calendars healthy, while for meat, and even cheese, it means that animals are fed in a more sustainable way and with better quality of food. Curious how to find out what is seasonal in Colorado? Check out the additional resources: You will find a link to a very useful calendar of Colorado produce and a second link to a seasonal food guide, where you can find specific information for any kind of food by state and season. 

Buying local and in season, and using labels and certifications to help your food choices are the basic steps to shop in a more sustainable way. These are the first steps to cooking with a lower impact on the environment. Indeed, cooking at home, starting from fresh ingredients, is the best way to have control over the ingredients used for your meals, and to make sure that your food is prepared in a sustainable way. Whether you decide to focus on the amount of packaging, on the type and the quality of the ingredients, or the food waste you produce when cooking, cooking at home you can have a huge impact on the environment. And your health and wallet. 

But what about dining out? Are there ways to go to a restaurant (dining in or taking out) and be sustainable? Of course! Find restaurants who keep sustainability in mind and who treat their employees fairly, ask the right questions when you are there and keep your food waste to a minimum. Finding sustainable restaurants might be tricky. Even if lists like the Good Food Restaurant List (for restaurants that put their food dollars to work in building local economies) or restaurants that are Certified USDA Organic, Certified Green Restaurants, or participate in Zero FoodPrint exist, certifications for restaurants are still not so common. A good way to weigh the sustainability level of a restaurant is checking the menu: Are ingredients in season and is the menu varying during the year? Are there indications of their sources, for example local farms or producers? Are there specifications on the meat they use, like grassfed, organic or from Colorado? You can normally find this information on the website of the restaurant or asking directly when there. Normally locally owned restaurants have the tendency to keep in mind sustainability more than national or international chains, and the small size restaurants might have more attention for the quality of their ingredients and the way they treat their employees. A good example of that are the restaurants part of the Three Leaf Farm system. Take a look at the menus of some of their restaurants!

If you are specifically looking for vegan or vegetarian options, the Happy Cow is one of the most convenient apps I’ve come across.

When you are at the restaurant, keep in mind the following:

  • Consider what is in season when ordering your meal. 
  • Give plant based dishes a try. 
  • If you eat fish, take a look at the Seafood Watch, an app born out of the Monterey Bay aquarium to help people eat seafood, understanding which types of fish (and from which geographic location) are considered sustainable. 
  • Consider the size of the portions and order only what you can finish, or at least take your leftovers home (ideally in your own container) and use them for the next meal. 
  • Don’t be afraid to ask for modifications if you don’t like part of the dish proposed or if it comes with unnecessary waste, like condiments that you don’t use (mayonnaise, ketchup, seasonings) or straws in your drinks. 
  • Order tap water and finish your glass before leaving. 
  • Refuse disposable cutlery and use your own reusables (ask me for an amazing bamboo set if you don’t have one yet!). 

Curious to know more about this topic? Here are some useful resources.

  • This is the complete calendar of produce in Colorado. You can use it to guide your choices at the supermarket. Have a specific type of food in mind and you don’t know when it is in season? Find information about it using this seasonal food guide.

  • Need tips on how to cook in a more sustainable way? Take a look at the input given by the foodprint webpage. They have information on the best way to store food, how to use the entire product, minimizing food waste and special creative tricks to use your food in the best way. As an example, check out all that you can do with sweet corn!

  • How Good's app helps you make better choices at the grocery store. Their algorithm rates products on 60 indicators of sustainability. To do this, they source information from over 350 organizations that include Humane Certified, Rainforest Alliance, and Fair Trade USA. With over 200,000 rated products, you can sort by the rankings ‘best,’ ‘great,’ and ‘good.’

  • Want to know more about food and sustainability? This is the page about food from Boulder city. Full or interesting resources, videos, and links. Especially interesting is the Climate Friendly Food Guide. 

 
TIPS OF THE WEEK

Want to be more sustainable with your food choices? Here are some suggestions on how to be more effective:

  1. Buy in season. Take a look at the resources given about to learn what is in season in Colorado. 
  2. Cook more at home. If you love cooking, it can become a hobby or even a couple or group activity to engage with your partner or friends: A pizza night can start with making the pizza or a date night with cooking fancy food together. If cooking is just not your thing, there are plenty of resources to learn to prepare a meal in a very efficient and fast way. Even if it is normally considered very time consuming, there are plenty of delicious meals you can cook in less than half an hour! 
  3. Choose the most sustainable ingredients and learn new ways to reduce food waste. 
  4. When eating out, keep in mind the suggestions for choosing the restaurant, selecting your dish and taking care of your leftovers I listed in the topic of the week section.
  5. Calculate your foodprint. You can do it just for fun, to see how well you are doing, and you can use it to get suggestions on what and how to switch to more sustainable choices in your food habits. 
  6. Now that you know everything about the food system and you can make the most aware choices, take a look at this 4-weeks food challenge. You can decide to try all the suggestions for four week or pick a couple of them every week and implement them in your habits. Remember: making more sustainable choices when it comes to food has an enormous impact on the environment!
  7. September is the Hunger Action Month. Learn more about it and consider it as an additional push to work on reducing your food waste this month!

GOOD NEWS OF THE WEEK

There is more than one good news this week. I couldn’t pick one of the three, so I am suggesting all.

College Students Revolutionize Food Delivery from Farms to Food Banks
Two college students used their technology know-how to help the Santa Monica Food Bank where they volunteered as children. As Covid-19 forced the economy to shut down, the West Side Food Bank in Santa Monica saw demand for food double overnight. Yet disruptions in the supply chain meant that getting the food became a challenge, NBC reports. These students noted that one-third of all food grown in the world goes to waste. In response to this, they started an initiative to go to the source. They called up at least 200 farms and were hung up on. Finally, they reached a farm that had no way of selling its food and had 18,000 eggs to give away. The students responded to this by creating a system, Farmlink, whereby farms could get food they couldn't sell to local food banks. From an initial group of five volunteers, they have now delivered over 9 million pounds of food to food banks and have grown to a volunteer crew of 180. Take a look at Take a look at this video.

Saving Food Using Solar Power
Due to out-dated supply-chain mechanisms and widespread power shortages, food storage is a major issue in Nigeria, resulting in significant food waste in an already impoverished country. But a local entrepreneur has solved this problem by installing walk-in cold storage rooms that are 100% solar-powered. His company has saved over 20,000 tons of food from getting spoiled, resulting in higher income for farmers and more food for the country. Read the story.

A Donations-Only Restaurant Spreads Joy in a Small Town
Drexell & Honeybee's is a little restaurant in a small Alabama town that serves anyone and everyone. It has no menu, no prices and no staff -- just a desire to feed the needy. The owners, Freddie and Lisa Thomas-McMillan, make no profit from their restaurant. One hundred percent of the donations customers make go back into serving people food. So what do they get from all this? The joy of serving others! "The more you do, the more you're able to do, and the richer you become. Not with things or money. With love," Lisa said.
