TOPIC OF THE WEEK: SUSTAINABLE HOLIDAYS

For many people the holidays are a time to get together with friends and family, celebrate with colleagues, and even exchange a gift or two, often involving a lot of food and sweets. Most of it may look different this year, with a lot of us not being able to travel back home and be with our families. And while this can be a joyful time to appreciate what we have and spend time with loved ones, it can also be an incredibly wasteful season! Being more aware of what we purchase during the holiday season is especially important: In the US, waste is increased by 25% between Thanksgiving and New Years. That is around 6 million tons of holiday waste being created this season. All of the added food waste, packaging, wrapping paper, ribbons, and more adds up to 1 million tons a week to our landfill. 1 million tons is a lot! How about this year we give the gift of reducing our holiday footprint in these easy ways?! In this newsletter, you can find suggestions on sustainable decorations, Christmas trees, and gift and gift packing ideas! Additionally, take a look at Eco-Cycle holiday guide, which outlines how to dispose of seasonally-specific items and gives additional ideas for how to avoid all that waste in the first place. 


While you're reading about seasonal Zero Waste tips, check out these two fun, kiddo-friendly videos on the subject: this one about green gift-giving, and this one about green Chanukah!

Sustainable Holiday Season
  • The holidays are a time to eat, drink and be merry. But during the holiday season 40% of the food we make is wasted. With just a bit of planning, people manage to indulge without food going to waste. Buying less and embracing ideas for leftovers on the following days seem to be the recipe for success. Keep in mind all the suggestions given in the Food Waste newsletter for the Holiday meals!
  • As for other meals, making your own dishes and avoiding pre-made food with wasteful packaging reduces significantly your impact. Have you checked ingredients from supermarket bulk sections? Or fully package free shops in Colorado? Ask me about possibilities in Boulder!
    • Never attempted making a Christmas pudding before? You might find you have a knack for it and it can become an amazing experience to share with your partner or your kids! And this year, you can even try to organize a virtual bake-off with your friends that live far away and share the meal preparation with them!
  • If you didn’t end up using all of your non-perishable items, donate them to a local food bank. Donating helps reduce holiday food waste and makes the holidays brighter for someone in need. https://www.foodpantries.org/ci/co-boulder is a great source for finding the food bank closest to you!
  • Carpool with a friend or take the bus when out holiday shopping. Heading to a holiday gift market or the nearest mall? Coordinate shopping plans with friends and family to help cut down on the amount of vehicles on the road. Consider checking which RTD buses are heading to your destination.
    • Not feeling safe with public transport this year? Bikes can take you everywhere in Boulder!
  • Perhaps you have string lights that no longer turn on or you’ve decided to replace your current holiday lights with the energy-saving LED version (LED lights not only last up to ten times longer, but use around 80% less energy than other incandescent lights).
    • Check for local organizations near your home that collect and recycle holiday lights: Eco-Cycle is the best option! You can also take old lights to your nearest hardware store such as McGuckins or Home Depot.
  • Turning off holiday lights when leaving a room, going to sleep or before you leave home helps save on energy costs and burns less fossil fuels. Set your holiday lights on a timer or plug them all into a single power strip to make shutting of lights a breeze. 
  • There are on average 2.65 billion Christmas cards sold each year in the US! This could fill a football field 10 stories high. Consider an ecard this year! Ecards are very fun, interactive, and best of all, cheap. But guess what? They also reduce paper waste and can save you money!  
Decorate Your Apartment
  • Decorate a house plant or wall instead of buying a tree. To cut costs and save resources, why not jazz up something that you already own? Add string lights and homemade ornaments to your indoor greenery or fashion your decorative supplies into a festive wall design.
    • Want some ideas on alternative trees? Take a look at those suggestions!
  • If you prefer to have a proper Christmas tree, opt for a real tree, better if Colorado grown (Reasons why I real tree is better than a fake one can be found here and here). They exist in different types, with tree farms that offer fresh-cut trees, live trees or even cut-your-own-tree experience.
    • Want to go more wild? The Forest Service gives permits to cut your own Christmas tree in a national forest, taking an active part in managing the national forest and having an amazing outdoor experience with your family! The permits are $5-$20 and can be purchased online.
  • Colorado’s abundant nature provides us with several options for DIY ornaments and decorations. Pine cones, flowers, twigs, berries, etc. can all be arranged into beautiful holiday decorations. If you are experiencing crafter's block, browse Pinterest for inspiration. Most of them can be reused next year and if you want to get rid of them, they are compostable!
    • Another amazing source of decoration options is food: Cookies and dry orange slices as tree ornaments, popcorn and cranberry tree garlands, cinnamon houses, orange peels or full oranges with cloves decorations, and so much more. And the best thing is that you can eat them after the Holidays!
  • Get crafty! The options are endless. Try to use recyclable material, the best option is using things you have at home: Toilet paper rolls can transform into cute snowflakes for your window, egg boxes make amazing Santa figures, newspapers are the perfect starting point for origami decorations, corks work beautifully as Christmas figures. Plenty of amazing ideas here.
  • If you are a more traditional type, hunt for ornaments in thrift stores. You can find unique beautiful items and you can keep them for years! 
Gift Giving
  • Gift an experience rather than an item. Instead of purchasing material items consider buying your loved ones tickets to an event or treating them to a relaxing experience. Some examples include: cooking classes, any online class, a massage appointment, or plan an adventure to the mountains!
    • Consider giving your time as well: You can cook dinner or go for a hike together, a game or a puzzle evening, a shared class. Also a CSA share (or part of it) or a gift card for a local restaurant could be amazing ideas! There is a place called Food Lab on Pearl Street which is doing in person and virtual cooking classes. Check it out. 
  • Make your own gifts. Get creative in the kitchen with edible gifts such as baked goods, nut mixes, jams/jellies, or a jar of loose tea. If cooking is not your forte, get crafty and knit or crochet a winter accessory, paint a picture, or create your own jewelry. Good options are also candles, cosmetics and toiletries (sugar or salt scrubs look amazing and smell even better!), homemade soup mixes, spice or salt rubs
    You can find also additonal amazing ideas for home made gift from your garden.
  • Give to a cause in the name of that person or name a star after that person. Instead of giving a gift this year, contribute to a cause that will support things your loved ones are passionate about: The WWF offers programs where you can symbolically “adopt” a wild animal, programs like adoptaclassroom.org allow you to support classrooms and teacher programs around the world. Star registry services such as starregister.org allow you to name a star and then give you the resources to locate that star in the night sky.
  • Consider giving a gift that is environmentally friendly. Shop local! Goods that are sourced locally travel fewer miles which creates fewer carbon emissions and support the local economy. Some other ideas for greener gifts include thrifting or purchasing items that are made with recycled material! Search for things that are plastic free and zero waste. Give things like a reusable water bottle, stainless steel food storage containers, or a watch made out of bamboo. Also do some research and try to buy from companies that work to reduce and offset the environmental impact of their shipping.
Gift Packing
  • Use gift bags/baskets over wrapping paper. Most wrapping paper is single-use and gift bags, baskets, boxes, and bows can be reused for years.
    • Pro tip: give gifts in reusable shopping bags. It will cut down on holiday waste and the recipient gets two gifts in one! Food storage containers and metal tins work great too, make the package a gift as well! 
  • Wrap gifts with discarded paper. Cleaning out your backpack at the end of the semester? Old assignments and handouts make for great wrapping paper! Newspapers, maps, posters, old calendars etc. are also excellent for wrapping. 
  • Print your own paper. If all this reusable paper is not festive enough, put some colors on! You can use biodegradable paints and your creativity: Super personalized wrapping!
  • Try Furoshiki. This is easy and one of the quickest forms of wrapping. There are many designs that are simple to follow and the clothes can be used over and over again both for gift-wrapping and for other uses. You can build up a set to use within the family and also make clothes to give away as part of gifts. Look out for square scarfs in op-shops, cut up old sheets and decorate them with natural dyes and printing. For more information check this document!
  • Decorate with t-shirt yarn. You can use it in place of ribbons and it looks fabulous, particularly next to brown paper and accompanied by a pom pom. If you've never made t-shirt yarn check out the instructions here. As an alternative, wool works perfectly well as well!
  • Children's paintings or drawing are also an amazing possibility for gift wrapping. And you can involve your little ones in the project. 
  • Get creative with reusing and repurposing. Keep old buttons, strings, beads, leaves, dry flowers, laces, fabric, jars, even take-out food boxes. Almost everything can become a creative gift package.
